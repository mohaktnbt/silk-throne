You wake to the call to prayer.

It comes from a minaret somewhere close — a voice like poured honey and broken glass, rising and falling through the grey-blue dawn. For a moment you do not know where you are. Then the silk sheets register against your skin, the scent of cold incense and marble finds your nose, and the weight of it settles back across your shoulders like a cloak.

You are the Grand Vizier. This is Khazara. And today you must survive the court.

Farid arrives before the sun has fully cleared the eastern wall, bearing a tray of black tea, dates, and a stack of documents that would have made your provincial steward weep.

"Good morning, Grand Vizier." He sets the tray down and begins laying papers across the desk in neat rows, like a general arranging troops. "I have taken the liberty of preparing a briefing. The situation is — " He searches for the word. "Delicate."

He pours your tea with hands that tremble slightly and begins.

The Audience Hall of the Silk Throne is a room designed to make mortals feel the proximity of heaven.

The ceiling is a dome of deep blue tile, set with golden stars in the precise arrangement of the night sky on the date of the empire's founding. The walls are carved marble screens through which the morning light filters in geometric patterns, so that the floor shifts and shimmers like the surface of a lake. At the far end, elevated on a dais of seven steps — one for each province of the empire — sits the Silk Throne itself: a chair of dark wood and ivory, its back woven with panels of shimmering silk in the imperial crimson.

And upon it sits a boy.

*page_break

Emperor Roshan is not what you expected. He is slight, dark-eyed, with the soft jawline of someone who has not yet finished growing into his face. He wears the imperial robes well enough — cloth-of-gold over white linen, a turban set with a single ruby the size of a quail's egg — but he wears them the way a child wears a parent's coat. The authority is borrowed.

And yet. When he sees you approaching across the hall, something flickers in those dark eyes. Not fear. Not arrogance. Something rarer in this palace.

Hope.

The audience continues with the usual business — trade disputes, provincial appointments, a border incident that requires delicate language. You observe. You learn. The Emperor defers to his ministers on details but occasionally interjects with a question that reveals a sharper mind than the court gives him credit for. The Dowager remains behind her screen, silent as a stone, but couriers come and go from her position with the regularity of a heartbeat.

After the audience, Farid appears at your elbow with three folded notes on a silver tray.

"You are popular, Grand Vizier," he says, without inflection. "Three dinner invitations for this evening."

He reads them aloud:

"The Dowager Empress Nur invites you to dine in her private apartments. She writes that she wishes to discuss 'the smooth continuation of governance.'"

"Guildmaster Zara invites you to the Merchant Quarter. She writes that she wishes to discuss 'the prosperity of the empire and mutually beneficial arrangements.'"

Farid folds the notes. "You can only accept one tonight. The others will take your refusal as a signal. Choose carefully."

The day passes in a blur of meetings, introductions, and the slow, suffocating weight of information. By the time the sun begins to set, painting the domes of Khazara in shades of copper and blood, you have begun to understand something your predecessor must have known.

This palace is not a seat of government. It is an arena. And every person in it is both gladiator and spectator, watching to see who will fall next.

The fountains murmur. The incense burns. And somewhere in the palace, someone is already planning your future — whether you will be a tool, an ally, or an obstacle to be removed.

Tonight, you dine with the first of them.
