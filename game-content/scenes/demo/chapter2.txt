The sun has set behind the western hills, and Khazara transforms. The marble corridors fill with lamplight — thousands of oil lamps in brass holders, casting the palace in a warm amber glow that softens every edge and deepens every shadow. Servants move through the halls in near-silence, bearing trays of sweetmeats and pitchers of sherbet. The fountains catch the lamplight and scatter it across the ceilings in rippling constellations.

You dress carefully. In the Khazaran court, clothing is a language, and tonight you are making a first argument.

  The Officers' Terrace sits atop the military wing of the palace, open to the sky. Torches flicker in iron brackets, and the smell of roasting meat hits you before you reach the top of the stairs — lamb on spits, whole chickens rubbed with cumin and chili, skewers of marinated goat. This is not the delicate cuisine of the inner court. This is soldiers' food, and a great deal of it.

  General Kadir waits at the head of a long trestle table, a cup of ayran in his fist rather than wine. He is enormous — not fat, but built like a siege tower, with shoulders that strain the seams of his military tunic and a beard so thick and black it looks like it could stop a blade. A scar runs from his left temple to his jaw, and his eyes are the pale grey of northern stone.

  *page_break

  "Vizier," he says. Not "Grand Vizier." Already a statement. "Sit down. Eat. I don't trust a man who won't eat."

  "The Turani are raiding again. Three villages burned last month. Sixty dead. I have forty thousand men sitting on the border playing dice because the treasury says we can't afford a campaign." He chews. "I don't need the treasury's permission to defend the empire. I need a Grand Vizier who understands that walls are maintained with steel, not with ledgers."

  The Merchant Quarter lies beyond the palace walls, and stepping into it is like crossing into another country. Where the palace is silence and marble, the Quarter is noise and color — even at night, lanterns sway over the narrow streets, and the coffeehouses are packed with merchants arguing over contracts in six languages.

  Guildmaster Zara's residence is an exception to the Quarter's chaos. It is a compound of elegant restraint — whitewashed walls, a courtyard garden of jasmine and night-blooming cereus, and a silence enforced not by emptiness but by very discreet guards. You count eight before you reach the dining room. There are certainly more.

  *page_break

  Zara herself is not what you expected. She is small — barely five feet — with silver-streaked hair worn in a simple braid and the calloused hands of someone who once loaded cargo herself. Her clothes are fine but not ostentatious: cream linen, a single gold bracelet. She does not need to display her wealth. Everyone already knows.

  "Grand Vizier. Welcome to my home." Her voice is crisp, her accent carrying the lilting cadence of the coastal provinces. "I apologize for the simplicity of the setting. I find the palace overwhelming. All that marble. One begins to feel like a fly in a temple."

  "These were found in your predecessor's study after his death," she says quietly. "My agents retrieved them before the Dowager's people could. They are Turani coins. Gold. Freshly minted."

You return to your chambers late, your mind churning with what you have learned. The palace is quiet now — the deepest quiet, the one that comes in the hours between midnight and dawn when even the fountains seem to hold their breath.

Farid is waiting in your anteroom, a single candle burning on the desk beside him. He stands when you enter.

"How was the dinner, Grand Vizier?"

You tell him what you learned. His face pales.

*page_break

"Grand Vizier." The voice is a woman's — low, measured, with the flatness of someone who has learned to keep all emotion locked behind the teeth. "Forgive the intrusion. I would not come at this hour without cause."

"Who are you?" Farid demands, his hand reaching for the bell that would summon the guards.

"Leave the bell," the woman says. There is no threat in her voice, but Farid's hand stops.

She steps forward, just enough for the candlelight to catch the edge of her face. Dark skin, high cheekbones, eyes that miss nothing. You have never seen her before.

"My name does not matter tonight. What matters is this." She looks directly at you, and her next words fall into the silence like a stone dropped into still water.

"Your predecessor didn't just die. He was murdered. And the killer is still in the palace."

Before you can respond, she turns and vanishes into the dark corridor, silent as smoke.

Farid is staring at the empty doorway. The candle gutters in a draft that was not there a moment ago.

The fountains murmur below.

The game has changed.
