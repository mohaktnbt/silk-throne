*comment === OPENING: THE WAR COUNCIL ===

The Council Chamber has been transformed.

The round table -- that elegant symbol of equality among the Council of Five, where you first witnessed the faction leaders argue and scheme and betray -- is gone. In its place stands a broad map table that General Kadir's aides carried in before dawn, its heavy oak legs scarring the marble floor. No one complained. The time for protecting marble floors is past.

The map of the empire sprawls across the table's surface -- hand-painted on lambskin by the imperial cartographers, every river and mountain and road marked in ink and gold leaf. Cities are tiny squares of red paint. Forests are strokes of green. The Ashghar Mountains rise in miniature ridges of brown, and the Jade Sea is a wash of pale blue at the eastern edge.

Wooden blocks represent armies: the empire's forces in white, the Khanate horde in black, Kaveh's supporters in red. The blocks are arranged according to the latest intelligence, and the picture they paint is stark.

There are far too many black and red blocks. Far, far too many.

Oil lamps burn at each corner of the table, their flames steady in the still air, casting the faces of your allies in warm, unsteady light that makes every expression a study in shadow and gold. The room smells of lamp oil, leather, and the bitter tang of the strong coffee that someone -- probably Farid -- has brewed in a large copper pot set on the side table.

These are the people who will determine the empire's fate. Not all the people you would have chosen. Not all the people you hoped for. But the people who are here.

Emperor Roshan enters last.

The door opens. Every head turns. Every conversation dies. The silence is not deference -- it is recognition. Everyone in this room has known the Emperor as a boy. They are about to see something else.

He has changed. You see it in the set of his shoulders -- squared, steady, no longer braced against the expectation of failure. In the way he crosses the room without hesitation and takes his place at the head of the table without waiting for invitation. In the clarity of his eyes, which are the same dark brown they have always been but which now carry something new behind them. Steel. Purpose. The resolve of a person who has been told the worst truth of his life and decided to stand anyway.

He is still sixteen. He is still the boy who asked you, on a terrifying night months ago, "What should I do?" But the boy has been forged, the way iron is forged -- in heat, under pressure, through pain. What remains is harder. Cleaner. Not yet a great emperor, perhaps. But something that could become one.

He wears plain military clothing -- dark fabric, leather belt, practical boots. No crown. No ceremonial robes. The only ornament is the imperial signet ring, and he wears it like a weapon rather than a decoration.
