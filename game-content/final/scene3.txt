*comment === OPENING: DAWN AND THE COUNCIL CHAMBER ===

Dawn comes to Khazara in layers.

First, the call to prayer. It rises from a hundred minarets in a wave of sound that breaks across the rooftops like surf, each voice joining the next in a cascade that builds from a single thread to a wall of sacred music. The sound fills the Citadel, pouring through every window and doorway and latticed screen, until even the stones seem to vibrate with it. You lie in bed and listen, and for a moment, the politics and the danger and the anonymous note hidden in the tax law volume seem very far away.

Then the light. It seeps through the latticed shutters in thin blades of gold, cutting the dark into geometric patterns that shift and slide across the floor as the sun climbs. The patterns are beautiful -- small gardens of light, growing and transforming on the silk carpets.

Then the smell of the city waking: bread baking in the palace kitchens, a warm, yeasty scent that drifts up from the courtyard below. Cook-fires being lit in the servants' quarters. The Tavan River giving up its cool morning mist, carrying with it the mineral cleanness of mountain water. And underneath everything, the faint, ever-present perfume of incense -- sandalwood and frankincense, the Citadel's constant companion, as much a part of the air here as oxygen.

You are dressed and waiting when Farid arrives. He has dark circles under his eyes and a stack of briefing papers so thick it requires both hands to carry.

"The Council Chamber is on the second floor of the eastern wing," he says, dealing papers onto your desk like a merchant laying out wares. "It overlooks the Garden of Eternal Spring. The table is round -- symbolically, all council members are equal." He pauses. "In practice, no one in that room has been equal to anyone else for a single moment of their lives. The seating arrangement is itself a political statement. I've drawn a diagram."

He produces a diagram. It is meticulously labeled and slightly coffee-stained.

"General Kadir sits with his back to the window -- he says it prevents anyone from reading his documents over his shoulder. Guildmaster Priya sits opposite, near the door, which she claims is for convenience but which also gives her the best exit route. High Priest Ashoka sits facing the east, toward the Temple Quarter. Spymaster Zara sits wherever she pleases, which is usually wherever no one expects her."

He looks up.

"The Vizier's chair is at the head of the table. Or the point. It's a round table, so geometrically there is no head, but -- you understand."

He shuffles through his papers one more time, then stops. His expression changes -- the nervous energy draining away, replaced by something steadier and more serious.

"${title_honorific} ${player_name}. I need to tell you something." He lowers his voice. "The previous Vizier -- Vizier Cyrus -- chaired his first Council meeting three years ago. He told me afterward that it was the most important hour of his tenure. Not because of the decisions made, but because of what he observed. He said the Council is not a meeting. It is a performance. Everyone in that room is performing for everyone else, and the Vizier's job is to see past the performance."

He pauses.

"He was very good at that. Seeing past performances. I think that might be why --" He stops himself. "I think that is a useful skill to have."

The unfinished sentence hangs in the air between you. You both know what it contains: the name of a dead man, and the reason for his death. Farid's eyes are bright with something that could be grief or could be fear or could be both, braided together so tightly that even he cannot separate them. You reach out and touch his arm -- briefly, a gesture of reassurance that crosses the boundary between superior and subordinate.

"I will be careful, Farid," you say.

He nods. Swallows. Adjusts his satchel strap with the fidgeting energy of a man who converts emotion into motion.

"The coffee is good in the antechamber," he says. "I've been there since five o'clock. I may have had too much. My hands are shaking, but I believe it's the caffeine rather than the terror." He manages a weak smile. "Mostly the caffeine."

*comment === THE COUNCIL CHAMBER ===

The Council Chamber is smaller than you expected. After the vast theater of the Jade Throne Room, this room feels almost intimate -- a space designed not for ceremony but for work. The round table dominates the center, its surface scarred with ink stains and the ghost impressions of maps long since removed. The chairs are heavy, practical, and worn smooth by generations of powerful occupants.

Three walls are hung with maps: the empire in its entirety, the northern frontier in detail, and the western mountain passes. The fourth wall is a window, floor to ceiling, overlooking the Garden of Eternal Spring. Morning light pours through it, catching dust motes in the air and illuminating the room with a clean, unflinching clarity.

The air smells of old paper, sandalwood incense, and something sharper -- tension, perhaps, or the memory of arguments that have soaked into the walls over centuries.

The council members are already assembled. They have arranged themselves around the table with the studied casualness of people who have been fighting over seating for years.

The fifth chair -- the Lord Speaker's chair -- is draped in black silk, the traditional sign of mourning for a vacant seat. Above the gallery, Lord Reza sits in an ornamental balcony reserved for observers and petitioners. He is seventy-two years old, white-haired, thin as a blade, and leaning on an ivory cane with the air of a man who has watched fools come and go for five decades and finds each new crop slightly more disappointing than the last. His eyes fix on you as you enter, and the assessment in them is neither hostile nor friendly. It is the assessment of a man who has survived by knowing, immediately and accurately, the precise value of every person in every room.

As you enter, four pairs of eyes at the table turn toward you simultaneously.

Each pair sees something different. The general sees a civilian. The guildmaster sees a variable. The priest sees a soul. The spymaster sees a piece on a board. You are all of these things and none of them, and the trick -- the trick that the last Vizier mastered and that you must learn -- is to be the version each of them needs, without losing the version you actually are.

"Vizier," he says. Not "welcome." Not "good morning." Just the title, spoken like a rank he has not yet decided to respect. His voice is the voice of a man who has given orders in sandstorms and been heard through the crash of cavalry charges. He turns back to his map, and the dismissal is so complete that you almost feel the air close behind it. You have been acknowledged. You have not been accepted. The difference, in General Kadir's world, is measured in blood and years and fortresses held against impossible odds, and you have supplied none of these credentials.

Behind you, the Council Chamber door swings shut with a sound like a book closing -- the soft, final thud of a chapter ending and another beginning. The symbol waits in the dark beneath the table, patient as a buried seed, carved into the wood by a hand you have not yet identified. It will be there tomorrow, and the day after, and the day after that. It will be there when the council meets to discuss the next crisis, and the one after that. It will be there when you sit in the Vizier's chair and make the next impossible decision, and the one after that.

The game has begun. The pieces are on the board. And somewhere in the palace, the spider waits at the center of its web, watching you learn the shape of the cage you have entered.

The conspiracy is real. Your predecessor found it and died. Now you have found its mark, scratched into the wood of the table where the empire's most powerful people sit and argue and scheme and -- perhaps -- betray.

The Eternal Flame burns in the Temple Quarter, visible through the window as a distant amber glow. The fountains murmur in the garden. The city hums with its two million lives, each one a thread in the vast, intricate tapestry of the Khazaran Empire. And somewhere in the pattern, a thread has been pulled loose. You can feel the fabric beginning to tear.

The question is no longer whether the threat exists.

The question is what you are going to do about it.
