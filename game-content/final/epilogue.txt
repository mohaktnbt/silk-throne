The Jade Throne Room is full. Not the tense, watchful fullness of the court you remember from your first year -- this is something warmer, louder, more alive. Courtiers mingle with merchants. Generals share cups of spiced wine with temple scholars. Children chase each other between the columns while their parents pretend not to notice. Secretary Farid -- now Secretary-General Farid, head of the imperial civil service -- stands near the door with a stack of documents that he has been told, firmly, to put down for one evening.

He has not put them down. Some things never change.

Emperor Roshan IV sits on the Jade Throne, and the throne fits him now. He is twenty-one, broad-shouldered, steady-eyed, with the quiet confidence of a man who has earned every ounce of respect he commands. The boy who trembled through his first audience is gone. In his place sits a ruler who quotes poetry with the ease of a scholar and reads military dispatches with the speed of a general. His wife -- a princess from the Southern Kingdoms, sealing a diplomatic marriage that ended three centuries of hostility -- sits beside him on a smaller chair of silver and ivory.

He catches your eye across the crowded room and raises his cup. The gesture is subtle -- no one else notices. But you see the warmth in it, the gratitude, the unspoken acknowledgment that this room, this empire, this peace was built by two people who trusted each other when trust was the most dangerous thing in the world.

You raise your cup in return.

Five years later, the report.

You sit in the Council Chamber, reading the quarterly assessment by lamplight. The numbers are good. Revenue is up twelve percent. The northern border is secure. Trade with the Western Confederation proceeds on schedule. The literacy program is ahead of projections. Secretary Farid has organized the civil service into a model of efficiency that other nations send delegations to study.

The numbers are good. The numbers are always good.

You pour yourself a cup of cardamom tea. It is cold. You did not notice it cooling.

*page_break

Emperor Roshan IV rules with competence and discipline. He is twenty-one now, married to a noblewoman selected by the Council for political advantage, and governing with the quiet efficiency of a man who has learned that ruling is not about inspiration but about execution. He makes sound decisions. He listens to his advisors. He does not inspire great love, but he does not provoke great hatred either.

You helped build this. The relationship between Vizier and Emperor is correct, professional, and meticulously maintained. He calls you "Vizier" in council and "Vizier" in private. You serve. He rules. The machine functions.

But there is a gap between you -- measured not in rank or protocol but in all the things that were never said, never trusted, never risked. The boy who asked you "What should I do?" with frightened eyes has become a man who no longer asks. Whether this is success or failure, you have not decided. You suspect you never will.

*page_break

The empire functions. General Kadir guards the border with mechanical precision, his reports arriving with the regularity of clockwork. Guildmaster Priya grows the economy with her usual brilliance, though she attends fewer state dinners now and more private ones. The warmth she once extended to you has cooled to professional cordiality. She senses the chill at the center of the empire and has drawn her own warmth inward.

High Priest Ashoka tends the Flame. He has grown thinner, older. His sermons speak more often of mercy now, and less of judgment. When he looks at you across the Council table, you see something in his eyes that might be concern, or pity, or the gentle sorrow of a man who has watched too many people mistake efficiency for purpose.

Spymaster Zara functions as she always has. She is the one member of the Council who seems unchanged by the years, because Zara was always cold, always precise, always operating at the exact temperature required. For her, the Iron Throne is simply business as usual.

*if (rel_farid > 60)

  "My lord Vizier," he says, setting down a cup of fresh cardamom tea you did not ask for. "You should sleep. The reports will still be terrible in the morning."

  You almost smile.

*page_break

The Dowager lives in comfortable exile. She writes no letters. She receives no visitors except a priest who comes on holy days. The empire has moved past her, and she has the dignity not to pretend otherwise. But sometimes, at night, you think of her words: "The throne is a cage made of gold." She was right about more things than you want to admit.

The city prospers, after a fashion. The Grand Bazaar has expanded. New bridges span the Ashar. The aqueducts carry water to the outer districts. But the celebrations that once filled the streets during the Festival of Lights are quieter now, more orderly, less spontaneous. The city has taken on the character of its government: functional, efficient, and carefully regulated.

Lady Soraya left the capital last year. She writes pamphlets about reform from a printing house in the provinces. The pamphlets are popular. You have not banned them. Banning them would require acknowledging they matter, and the Iron Throne does not acknowledge things that matter. It acknowledges things that function.

Lord Reza died in his sleep at the age of seventy-seven. His eulogy praised two hundred years of noble service. You attended. You stood in the back. You felt nothing in particular, which was itself a kind of feeling.

Just the machinery of state, turning and turning, day after day, year after year, grinding grain into bread and taxes into roads and silence into policy.

The Eternal Flame burns in the temple, as it always has, as it always will. And you endure beside it, steady and cold and necessary, like the iron bones beneath the jade.

The empire does not celebrate. The empire does not mourn. The empire simply endures. And so do you.

You stand before a stone marker in the Garden of Eternal Spring. The fountain still runs, but one of its jets is blocked, and the water pattern that once formed a perfect geometric star now sprays in a lopsided circle. Small things. The sort of things that go unrepaired when an empire is spending all its energy just staying upright.

Six months later, the trial.

You stand in the Jade Throne Room in chains. The iron is cold on your wrists. The court that once bowed to the Voice of the Throne now watches you with the hungry, horrified fascination of people witnessing a disaster they always suspected was coming.

The gallery is packed. They came early to get good seats. The fall of a Grand Vizier is the most exquisite entertainment the court has known in a generation, and the court of Khazara has always loved its entertainments.

*page_break

The evidence is laid out on a marble table before the throne. Every secret deal. Every broken promise. Every moment where you chose expedience over honor and then lied about the choosing. The prosecutor -- a thin-voiced magistrate with spectacles and a meticulous memory -- reads your own letters aloud, and the words fill the Throne Room like smoke from a fire that has been smoldering for a very long time.

Emperor Roshan IV sits on the Jade Throne. He does not look at you.
