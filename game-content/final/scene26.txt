*comment === OPENING: THE EVIDENCE ASSEMBLED ===

The evidence is laid out on the Council Chamber table like a surgeon's instruments before an operation. Coded letters, decoded and annotated in three colors of ink. Forged seals, identified and matched to their official counterparts. Testimony from captured agents, transcribed in Zara's meticulous hand -- each page signed and witnessed, each confession taken separately, each detail cross-referenced against the others.

Financial records showing diverted treasury funds -- thousands of gold ashrafs, siphoned through a labyrinth of false accounts and phantom merchants, funneled to agents in the Old City and handlers in the Foreign Quarter. Troop rotation schedules that match, date for date, hour for hour, the intelligence leaked to the Northern Khanates. A letter, unsigned but unmistakable in its phrasing, that authorized the murder of your predecessor.

You stand at the head of the table and look at the proof of betrayal, and you feel nothing.

No -- that is not true. You feel everything. But the feelings are so vast, so tangled -- rage and grief and disbelief and a terrible, aching disappointment -- that they cancel each other out into a humming numbness, like the silence after a great bell has been struck. Like the emptiness at the center of a whirlpool.

The Council Chamber is empty except for you and Zara. The round table, where factions have argued and alliances have been forged and broken, is covered in paper instead of politics. The morning light falls through the high windows and illuminates the evidence with an almost sacred glow, as though the documents are holy texts.

They are, in a way. They are the sacred texts of betrayal.

*page_break

Zara stands at the window, her back to you. Dawn light traces the sharp lines of her profile -- the strong jaw, the cropped hair, the stillness that most people mistake for calm but which you now recognize as a form of compression. She holds herself tight because the alternative is to explode.

"The evidence is conclusive," she says. Her voice is the voice she uses for intelligence briefings: flat, precise, stripped of everything except information. "Three independent sources confirm the identity. The agents we captured have corroborated under separate interrogation, in different cells, questioned by different people, with no opportunity to coordinate their stories."

She turns. Her eyes are steady, but you know her well enough now -- after months of shared danger and cautious trust and the slow, careful building of something that is not quite friendship but is close enough to hurt -- to see what lies beneath that professional mask. The weight of what she is about to ask you to do. The recognition that this will break something that cannot be repaired.

"You need to confront them. Today. Before they learn their network has been taken apart. Before they can run, or destroy evidence, or -- " She pauses. Chooses the word. "-- prepare."

The word *prepare* carries a specific meaning in Zara's vocabulary. It means: *before they can marshal forces to fight back.*

The Dowager's Wing smells of oud and dark roses. It always does. The heavy silk curtains filter the morning light into shades of amber and red, so that every room feels like the interior of a jewel -- or a heart.

She is waiting for you. Of course she is. The Dowager Empress Nur has survived thirty years of court politics by never being surprised.

She sits in her high-backed chair, dressed in imperial violet, her silver hair arranged with the precision of a woman who treats her appearance as statecraft. A tray of untouched tea sits beside her. The incense in the corner brazier sends a thin thread of smoke toward the ceiling.

"Vizier." Her voice is calm. Measured. The voice that has counseled and controlled and commanded for three decades. "You have that look. The one people wear when they carry bad news and worse decisions."

*page_break

You set the evidence on the table between you. The coded letters. The decoded transcripts. The testimony. The forged seals. The financial records tracing diverted treasury funds to agents in the Old City and beyond.

"Tell me," she says. "In your own words."

"You are the architect of the conspiracy against the throne. You leaked military intelligence to the Northern Khanates. You funded Prince Kaveh's claim. You arranged the murder of my predecessor when he discovered your network."

"Their deaths are on your hands."

*page_break

The Dowager Empress Nur closes her eyes. When she opens them, the mask is gone. Not stripped away -- set aside. Deliberately. She lets you see, for the first time, the face beneath the empress.

She is tired. Bone-deep tired, in the way of a woman who has carried an empire's weight for thirty years and cannot remember what it felt like to set it down.

"I did this for the empire."

The words hang in the air like smoke.

"My son is a good boy. A kind boy. An intelligent boy." Her voice does not waver. "He is not a strong emperor. He will not become one. I have watched him for sixteen years, and I know -- the way a mother knows, the way the river knows the shape of its banks -- that Roshan will be consumed by this throne."

*page_break

She rises. Moves to the window. Looks out over the city she has helped rule for three decades.

"The empire needs strength. Unshakable, unyielding strength. Not the kind that comes from a boy who reads about great rulers and tries to imitate them. The kind that comes from certainty. From knowing what must be done and doing it, regardless of cost."

"Kaveh would be your puppet."

"Kaveh would be *guided.* As Roshan should have been guided. As I tried to guide him, before you--" She stops. Composes herself. "Before you taught him to stand on his own."

The accusation is breathtaking. She blames you -- not for opposing her, but for *succeeding.* For doing the very thing she could not: helping Roshan become a ruler.

*page_break

"You made him strong enough to refuse me," she says quietly. "That is either your greatest achievement or your greatest crime. I have not yet decided which."

Lord Reza's estate is the oldest private residence in Khazara -- a sprawling compound of dark stone and ancient cedar, its gardens overgrown with two centuries of aristocratic cultivation. The servants bow as you enter, but their eyes are wary. They know their master receives visitors like a king holds court: with ceremony, hierarchy, and the implicit understanding that you are in *his* domain.

He receives you in his study -- a room lined with books older than the empire's current dynasty, the air thick with the smell of leather and lamp oil. A portrait of Reza's grandfather, who served Roshan II, dominates one wall. The family resemblance is striking: the same hawk nose, the same imperious gaze, the same absolute certainty of one's own importance.

Lord Reza sits behind a desk that could double as a fortification. He does not rise.

*page_break

"Vizier." He says the title the way one might say *insect* -- with precise, controlled distaste. "An unexpected visit. I was not aware we had an appointment."

"We don't. I find that appointments give people time to prepare. I prefer honest conversations."

"Honest." The old man's lip curls. A slight gesture, almost elegant in its contempt. "An interesting word, from a person in your position."

You lay the evidence before him. Letters. Decoded ciphers. Financial records. Testimony from his own agents, captured in the night.

He reads them. Slowly. With the calm of a man examining a contract rather than his own destruction.

*page_break

When he finishes, he sets the papers down and folds his hands. His signet ring -- the Reza family crest, older than the Silk Throne itself -- catches the light.

"I did this for the empire."

The words are measured, deliberate, and utterly without apology.

"For three hundred years, the noble houses have been the empire's spine. We built it. We bled for it. We held it together through civil wars, invasions, plagues. And now -- now a boy sits on the throne, advised by a provincial nobody, and the foundations crack."

He stands. Despite his seventy-two years, his back is straight as a spear shaft.

*page_break

"You think you have uncovered a conspiracy. You have uncovered a *correction.* The empire's natural order has been disrupted -- by the Emperor's weakness, by the Dowager's meddling, by *your* presumption -- and I have merely attempted to restore it."

"By leaking intelligence to the Khanates? By letting soldiers die?"

"By demonstrating that the empire cannot defend itself without the nobility's full commitment. Every raid, every breach, every embarrassment -- all of it proof that the old system works and your innovations do not."

The arrogance is breathtaking. And yet you see, beneath it, the genuine conviction of a man who has believed his entire life that birth determines worth, that the ancient families are the empire's truest guardians.

You do not go alone.

And behind them, a dozen minor nobles, priests, and officers who have staked their futures on the old order's survival.

*page_break

They see you enter. The room goes quiet the way a forest goes quiet when a predator appears.

"Ladies and gentlemen," you say. "This literary society has an unexpected guest."

Reza's voice cuts through the silence like a blade. "The Vizier graces us with a visit. How delightful. I was not aware we had extended an invitation."

"You didn't. Consider my presence a response to the invitations you *have* extended -- to the Northern Khanates, to Prince Kaveh's supporters, to anyone who might help you destroy the reforms this empire desperately needs."

"Per regulation 201 of the Imperial Code, these documents constitute formal notification of charges under the Treason Statutes, Section--"

"Treason." Elder Vahid's voice booms through the hall, rich with temple authority. "The Vizier speaks of treason. The sacred laws of this empire were written to protect the faithful from *precisely* this kind of tyranny."

*page_break

"The sacred laws of this empire were written to protect all its people," you reply. "Not merely those born to privilege."

The room erupts. Not into violence -- not yet -- but into the furious, overlapping arguments of powerful people confronting the end of their world. Reza pounds the table. Vahid invokes the Eternal Flame. Rostam fingers his sword hilt. Tahir edges toward the door.

You let them rage. You stand in the center of it, the calm eye of a storm, and wait.

When the noise subsides -- not into silence, but into a seething, watchful tension -- Lord Reza speaks for them all.

"We did this for the empire."

*page_break

The words are the same. The meaning is different. When the Dowager might have said them with a mother's fierce conviction, and Reza alone might have said them with an aristocrat's arrogance, the coalition says them with the desperate certainty of people who believe -- truly, genuinely believe -- that they are the last defense against chaos.

"The empire has endured for three centuries," Reza continues, "because of order. Because of hierarchy. Because every person knew their place and the system -- however imperfect -- *worked.* You come from the provinces and you see injustice. We have lived here for generations and we see *stability.* You would tear it apart for an ideal. We would preserve it because ideals do not feed people. Systems do."

Vahid nods. "The Flame teaches that change without wisdom is merely destruction wearing a mask."

Rostam says nothing. His hand has not left his sword.
