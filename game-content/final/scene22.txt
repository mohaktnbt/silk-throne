*comment === OPENING: THE MARCH NORTH ===

The road north from Khazara runs straight as a spear shaft through the Valley of the Ashar, cutting between rice paddies and cotton fields that shimmer in the late-autumn sun. The harvest is nearly in -- bundles of grain stacked in golden rows along the field edges, cotton bolls bursting white against the dark earth -- and the sight of it, this abundance, this wealth that feeds two million people, makes the urgency of the march physical. This is what the Khanates are riding toward. These fields. This food. This beating heart of the empire.

For two days you ride at the head of the relief column -- twelve thousand soldiers, the best the capital garrison could spare without leaving the city defenseless. They march in formation despite the road dust and the autumn heat, their armor bright, their standards snapping in the wind. They are disciplined, well-trained, and afraid. You can see it in the way they check their weapons at every rest stop, the way conversations die when the wind carries the smell of smoke from the north, the way the youngest soldiers -- boys and girls barely older than the Emperor -- look at the horizon with eyes that are trying very hard to be brave.

The countryside tells its own story as you ride north. The further from the capital, the darker the tale.

In the first hours, the road is normal -- farmers at their work, merchants with their carts, children chasing goats in the ditches. But by midday of the first day, the refugees appear. They come south in a ragged stream along the road's shoulders, families with their possessions piled on oxcarts that sag under the weight of everything a household values: cooking pots, blankets, bags of grain, a carved wooden chest, a cage of chickens, the family dog trotting alongside with its tongue out. Their faces are grey with dust and fatigue, and their eyes, when they look at the soldiers, hold a complicated mixture of hope and accusation: where were you when we needed you?

An old man stands at a crossroads, blessing each passing rider with a gesture toward the Eternal Flame -- touching his forehead, his heart, and the air above him. His lips move in prayer. He has been standing there, you learn from a local militiaman, since dawn yesterday. He will not move. He will not eat. He stands and blesses, and the soldiers ride past him with bowed heads.

By the second day, the refugees are more numerous and more desperate. Women carrying children on their hips, grandparents leaning on walking sticks, young men with bandages that speak of Khanate arrows and close-quarter combat. The road becomes a river flowing in two directions -- soldiers north, civilians south, neither slowing for the other.

On the third morning, you see the smoke.

It rises from the northern horizon in a black column, thick and greasy, fed by something more substantial than wood. Buildings. Villages. The thatched roofs and wooden beams of farmhouses that have stood for generations, now feeding a fire that can be seen for thirty miles. The Khanates have reached the inner provinces, and they are burning what they find. Not from malice, necessarily -- burned villages are denied to retreating armies, and the smoke signals to the surrounding population that resistance is futile. It is strategy, not cruelty. The relief column picks up its pace without being ordered. You feel the change in the soldiers around you -- a tightening, a quickening, the collective intake of breath that precedes the plunge. Conversations die mid-sentence. Hands find sword hilts and bowstrings with the unconscious automatism of training. The column moves in near-silence, the only sounds the creak of leather, the clink of armor plates, the rhythm of twenty-four thousand hooves on packed earth, and the distant, terrible murmur of smoke on the horizon.

*comment === FIRST REAL CHOICE: MILITARY STRATEGY ===

Kadir's plan is simple in concept and terrifying in execution.

"We cannot defend the entire wall. It is too long and too damaged. The breaches are too wide to repair under enemy fire. Instead, we concentrate our forces at the central section -- the strongest remaining fortification, the section where the gate still holds and the walls are thickest -- and let the eastern and western breaches stand open."

"Open?" Farid's voice cracks like a dry twig.

"Open. Inviting. A gift. The Khanates will pour through the breaches, expecting minimal resistance -- because that is what their intelligence told them to expect, and their intelligence has been correct about everything so far. When their forces are committed -- when the main body of the army has entered the breach and moved south past the wall -- we close the trap." His iron finger traces two curving arrows on the map. "Cavalry from the west and infantry from the east, striking the enemy column from both flanks while the central wall holds in front of them. The breaches become a killing ground. The Khanates' mobility, their greatest advantage, is neutralized in the confined space."

"And if the trap fails?" you ask. "If the timing is wrong, or Borte Khan reads it?"

The silence that follows is not the silence of deliberation. It is the silence of people staring into an abyss and trying to decide whether to jump.

The Khanate army appears with the sunrise.
