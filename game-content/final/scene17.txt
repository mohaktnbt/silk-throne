Three days since the betrayal.

You know it is three days because Farid has been counting. You know it is morning because the light through the window has the pale, watered quality of early autumn -- thin as parchment, offering illumination without warmth. You know you are alive because the seal ring on your finger has a weight, and the dead, presumably, feel nothing at all.

Three days. In the empire's three-hundred-year history, three days is nothing -- less than nothing, a heartbeat between one era and the next. But in the span of a life, three days can be an epoch. Three days ago you were Grand Vizier of the Khazaran Empire, second in authority only to the Emperor himself, a person whose word moved armies and whose seal authorized the expenditure of millions. Now you are Grand Vizier in name only -- the title still yours, the power drained from it like wine from a cracked vessel.

You know it is three days because Farid has been counting. He marks each morning on a piece of parchment tucked into his ledger -- not with numbers but with small, precise tallies, the kind a prisoner scratches into a cell wall. You have not asked him to stop. The tallies are a kind of honesty in a world that has suddenly become dishonest -- a simple acknowledgment that time is passing, that the situation is not a dream from which you will wake, that each day you survive is a day earned and a day that must be endured.

Your chambers in the Citadel have not changed. The same marble floors, the same silk curtains, the same desk carved from Ashghar cedar. The Vizier's seal ring still sits on your finger. No one has come to take it. That is either a sign of hope or a sign that no one considers you worth the trouble.
