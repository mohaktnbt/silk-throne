*comment === OPENING: THE TEMPLE OF SEVEN WISDOMS ===

The Temple Quarter is quiet in a way that temples should never be quiet.

You have come here at every hour since your arrival in Khazara -- at dawn for the Morning Flame, at noon when the sun strikes the temple's copper domes and sets them ablaze with reflected light, at dusk when the incense thickens the air and the evening prayers echo through the seven chambers like the murmur of a river finding its course. At every hour, the Quarter has been alive with the particular vitality of sacred space: monks chanting the verses of the Seven Wisdoms in voices that have been trained since childhood to find the resonant frequencies of stone; temple guards pacing their posts in crimson cloaks, their spear points catching the light of oil lamps; pilgrims filing through the seven chambers with offerings of incense and oil and flowers, their faces soft with devotion. The Quarter has its own heartbeat, its own breath, its own warmth that radiates from the Eternal Flame at its center like heat from a living body.

Today the heartbeat falters.

The monks are silent. Not absent -- you can see them in the shadows of the colonnades, standing in clusters of two and three, speaking in whispers that die when you approach. Their faces are drawn, their eyes uncertain. The pilgrims are gone -- the Quarter's gates are open, but something in the air, some emanation of spiritual crisis that the faithful sense without being told, has kept them away. The temple guards stand at their posts with the rigid alertness of soldiers expecting an attack, and you realize, as you pass between them, that the attack they expect is not from without. The guards' eyes move not toward the Quarter's gates but inward, toward the temple's own halls.

The schism has come.

*page_break

The smell of the temple has changed. On an ordinary day, the air is thick with frankincense and sandalwood, the accumulated perfume of three centuries of worship layered into the very stone. Today, beneath the incense, there is something else -- something acrid and metallic, like the smell of a lamp burning the last of its oil. The smell of ending.

High Priest Ashoka waits for you in the Chamber of the First Wisdom -- Justice. It is the smallest of the seven chambers, barely large enough for a dozen people, its walls covered in geometric tilework of blue and gold that depicts the scales of balance, the symbol of the First Wisdom. A single lamp burns before the altar -- a small flame that seems, in the chamber's dimness, like a distant echo of the Eternal Flame that burns in the courtyard beyond. The incense here is frankincense, sharp and clean, cutting through the dusty air like a blade through silk.

Ashoka sits on a stone bench beside the altar. He is not praying. He is not meditating. He is staring at the wall with an expression you have never seen on his face before -- an expression that belongs not to the High Priest of the Eternal Flame, the spiritual authority of two million souls, the man who speaks with the voice of the divine, but to a human being confronting a problem that his faith, his learning, his sixty-two years of devoted service cannot solve.

He looks old. You have known Ashoka for months, and he has always seemed ageless -- a man whose spiritual vitality transcends the body, whose calm radiates outward like heat from the Flame itself. Today, the body has reasserted itself. His shoulders are bowed. The lines in his face have deepened from character marks to trenches. His hands, folded in his lap in the posture of meditation, are not still. They tremble.

"Vizier," he says. "Thank you for coming."

*comment === THE GUTTERING FLAME ===

You walk with Ashoka through the connecting passage that leads from the Chamber of the First Wisdom to the central courtyard of the Temple of Seven Wisdoms -- the heart of the Quarter, the heart of the faith, the spiritual heart of three centuries of empire.

The Eternal Flame burns in a great bronze brazier at the courtyard's center, sheltered by a canopy of hammered copper supported on eight pillars of carved stone. Each pillar represents one of the Seven Wisdoms -- seven pillars for the seven virtues, and the eighth for the Flame itself, which encompasses and transcends them all. The brazier is vast -- six feet across, its surface etched with prayers in Classical Parvani, its rim worn smooth by the hands of monks who have tended it since the empire's founding. For three hundred years, since Roshan the Conqueror lit it with a torch taken from the ruins of Old Parvana, the Flame has burned without interruption. Storms have not quenched it. Floods have not reached it. Earthquakes have shaken the ground beneath it and the Flame has burned on, serene and unchanging, a constant in a world of variables.

It is guttering.

*page_break

The word does not do justice to what you see.

The Flame, normally a steady column of gold and amber rising two feet from the brazier's oil, is dancing. Not the natural dance of a fire in wind -- there is no wind in the sheltered courtyard, no draft, no breath of air through the columns. The copper canopy is a windbreak, and the courtyard's walls deflect every current. The conditions are, and have been for three centuries, precisely controlled for the maintenance of a constant flame.

This is something else. The Flame shrinks -- contracting to half its normal height, the oil surface darkening as the fire draws inward -- then rises, surging up with a sudden flare that casts sharp shadows across the courtyard walls. It shrinks again. The color shifts from gold to orange to a deep, unsettling red that looks less like fire and more like the last light of a dying ember. Then back to gold. Then orange again. The rhythm is irregular, unpredictable, as though the Flame is breathing -- labored, uncertain breaths, the respiration of something alive and struggling.

The monks who tend the Flame -- three at all times, in eight-hour rotations, a tradition unbroken for three centuries -- stand at the brazier's edge with expressions of naked terror. Two of them are elderly, their faces etched with decades of devotion, their hands clasped in prayers that they have been reciting without pause for three days. The third is young -- a woman barely out of her novitiate, with dark eyes made huge by fear and the firelight -- and she is weeping silently, tears running down her cheeks unchecked.

"When did it begin?" you ask.

"Three days ago," Ashoka says. "The morning after the Khanate invasion was reported. The morning the broadsheets appeared. The morning the world changed." He pauses. "Some say it is a natural phenomenon -- the oil supply disrupted by the crisis, the wick degraded by the fluctuating temperatures of recent weeks. The temple's engineers have examined the brazier and found nothing wrong with its physical components."

"And the others?"

"The others say the Flame is dying because the empire has lost the favor of the divine. Because the throne is occupied by a usurper's descendant. Because the reforms -- your reforms, Vizier -- have angered the old gods by disturbing the natural order." He says this without accusation, without agreement. He says it as a fact, the way he says everything -- with the measured care of a man for whom truth is a sacred obligation.

*page_break

You stand before the Eternal Flame, and you feel -- something.

Not faith, exactly. Not the certainty that Ashoka carries, the deep knowing that has sustained him through four decades of spiritual service. Not the devotion of the weeping monk, whose world is built on the foundation of this fire and who watches that foundation shake. But something. The warmth on your face is more than heat. The light in your eyes is more than the physics of combustion. There is a weight to this place, a gravity that pulls at something inside your chest -- something primal, something older than reason, older than theology, older than the empire itself. Whether that gravity is divine or merely the accumulated belief of three centuries of worshippers, condensed into stone and flame and air until the very atoms vibrate with meaning, you cannot say.

But it is real. Whatever it is, it is real.

The Flame gutters. The shadows dance. The young monk weeps.
