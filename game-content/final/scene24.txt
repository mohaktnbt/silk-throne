*comment === OPENING: THE TEMPLE OF SEVEN WISDOMS ===

The Temple Quarter is quiet in a way that temples should never be quiet.

You have come here at every hour since your arrival in Khazara -- at dawn for the Morning Flame, at noon for the midday reflection, at dusk for the evening prayers. At every hour, the Quarter has been alive: monks chanting, temple guards pacing their posts, pilgrims filing through the seven chambers with offerings of incense and oil and whispered pleas. The Quarter has its own heartbeat, its own breath, its own warmth that radiates from the Eternal Flame at its center.

Today the heartbeat falters.

The monks are silent. The pilgrims are gone. The temple guards stand at their posts with the rigid alertness of soldiers expecting an attack, and you realize, as you pass between them, that the attack they expect is not from without. The guards' eyes move not toward the Quarter's gates but inward, toward the temple's own halls.

The schism has come.

*page_break

High Priest Ashoka waits for you in the Chamber of the First Wisdom -- Justice. It is the smallest of the seven chambers, barely large enough for a dozen people, its walls covered in geometric tilework of blue and gold. A single lamp burns before the altar. The incense here is frankincense, sharp and clean, cutting through the dusty air.

Ashoka sits on a stone bench beside the altar. He is not praying. He is staring at the wall with an expression you have never seen on his face before -- the expression of a man confronting a problem that his faith cannot solve.

He looks old. You have known Ashoka for months, and he has always seemed ageless -- the kind of man whose spiritual vitality transcends the body. Today, the body has reasserted itself. His shoulders are bowed. The lines in his face are trenches. His hands, folded in his lap, are not still. They tremble.

"Vizier," he says. "Thank you for coming."

*comment === THE GUTTERING FLAME ===

You walk with Ashoka to the central courtyard of the Temple of Seven Wisdoms -- the heart of the Quarter, the heart of the faith, the heart of three centuries of empire.

The Eternal Flame burns in a great bronze brazier at the courtyard's center, sheltered by a canopy of hammered copper supported on pillars of carved stone. For three hundred years, since Roshan the Conqueror lit it with a torch taken from the ruins of Old Parvana, the Flame has burned without interruption. Storms have not quenched it. Floods have not reached it. Armies have fought and died within sight of it, and it has burned on, serene and unchanging.

It is guttering.

*page_break

The word does not do justice to what you see. The Flame, normally a steady column of gold and amber rising two feet from the brazier's oil, is dancing. Not the natural dance of a fire in wind -- there is no wind in the sheltered courtyard. This is something else. The Flame shrinks, rises, shrinks again. Its color shifts from gold to orange to a deep, unsettling red, then back. Shadows leap across the courtyard walls in patterns that seem, if you watch them long enough, almost deliberate.

The monks who tend the Flame -- three at all times, in eight-hour rotations, a tradition unbroken for three centuries -- stand at the brazier's edge with expressions of naked terror. One of them, a young woman barely out of her novitiate, is weeping silently, her hands clasped in prayer.

"When did it begin?" you ask.

"Three days ago," Ashoka says. "The morning after the Khanate invasion was reported. Some believe it is a natural phenomenon -- the oil supply disrupted by the crisis, the wick degraded. Others believe --"

He does not finish. He does not need to.

Others believe the Flame is dying because the empire has lost the favor of the divine.

*page_break

You stand before the Eternal Flame, and you feel -- something.

Not faith, exactly. Not the certainty that Ashoka carries, the deep knowing that the Flame is more than fire, that it is the manifest presence of something vast and ancient and intentional. But something. The warmth on your face is more than heat. The light in your eyes is more than photons. There is a weight to this place, a gravity that pulls at something inside your chest, and whether that pull is divine or merely the accumulated belief of three centuries of worshippers, it is real.

The Flame gutters. Shadows dance. The young monk weeps.
