*comment === OPENING: MORNING AFTER ===

You wake to the smell of jasmine and smoke.

For a moment -- one merciful, stolen moment -- you do not remember. The morning light falls across your sleeping platform in warm bars of gold, and the garden fountains murmur their eternal conversation outside the window, and the air carries the faint sweetness of orange blossom from the trees that line the Citadel's inner courtyard. You are simply a person waking in a beautiful room in the greatest city in the world, and the world is whole, and the night was only a night.

Then memory returns, and the weight of it presses you back against the pillows like a physical force.

The Emperor. The blood on white marble -- Corporal Behzad's blood, still wet, still warm, pooling in the geometric channels between the tiles. The calling card with its terrible, familiar symbol. The longest night of the Khazaran Empire, measured in torchlight and whispers and the sound of a sixteen-year-old boy's voice trying not to break. The three paths that crystallized in your mind like frost on glass as the dawn crept across the city.

You lie still for a count of ten heartbeats, feeling the weight of the linen sheets and the press of the mattress beneath you and the seal ring -- always the seal ring -- heavy on your finger where you forgot to remove it before collapsing into bed. You slept three hours. Perhaps four. The exhaustion lives in your bones like an ache.

You rise. Your body protests -- muscles stiff from running through corridors, from kneeling on cold marble, from the particular tension that comes from holding yourself rigid through hours of crisis. The morning air from the window is cool against your skin, carrying jasmine and the distant tang of woodsmoke from the city's breakfast fires.

The seal ring sits on the bedside table where you placed it. It catches the sunlight, and the jade glows -- a deep, living green, like a forest pool, like a secret kept beneath the surface of the world. You pick it up. Slide it onto your finger. It fits now, in a way it did not when you first arrived in Khazara. The ring has not changed. You have. Months of court politics, of faction wars, of conspiracy and crisis -- they have shaped you the way a river shapes stone. Slowly, irrevocably, in ways you cannot fully see.

Farid arrives with breakfast.

The tray is laden with more care than usual. Flatbread, warm from the oven, its surface blistered and golden. Soft white cheese crumbled over a dish of olive oil and herbs. Honey in a small clay pot, dark and fragrant, the kind that comes from the mountain apiaries in the Ashghar range. Figs, split open to reveal their crimson centers. A pot of cardamom tea whose fragrance fills the room the moment Farid removes the lid -- warm, complex, grounding, the smell of every good morning you have ever had and some you have not yet earned.

Farid sets the tray down with his usual careful precision, adjusting the position of each dish as if he is arranging the components of a machine. Then he stands at attention with his hands clasped behind his back, his spectacles slightly crooked, his face a study in controlled anxiety.

He looks as though he has not slept at all. The skin beneath his eyes is dark, and his collar is rumpled -- a detail so unusual for Farid that it might as well be a scream.

"The situation, my ${title_honorific} Vizier," he begins, and you hear the faintest tremor in his voice -- the vibration of a string wound too tight. "General Kadir has tripled the Citadel guard. Every entrance and exit is manned by armed soldiers. The Dowager Empress has convened a private meeting of the noble houses for this afternoon -- invitations were sent before dawn, which suggests she had the guest list prepared in advance."

He pauses for breath. You pour tea. You listen.

"High Priest Ashoka is conducting prayers for the Emperor's safety at the Temple of Seven Wisdoms. The public has been told there was a 'disturbance in the imperial wing,' not a full assassination attempt, but rumors are spreading. The bazaar is full of talk. "Prince Kaveh?"

"The rumor does not specify, but the implication is... heavy." Farid consults a page of notes that is, you notice, stained with coffee. "Spymaster Zara has not been seen since before dawn. Her agents are moving through the Old City in significant numbers, but they will not say why or where she has gone. Guildmaster Priya sent a message asking whether the trade embargo negotiations should continue or be suspended -- her exact words were 'I need to know whether we're still selling silk or buying coffins.'"

Farid's expression softens. The anxiety does not leave, but something else surfaces alongside it -- tenderness, perhaps. Concern for a young man who was almost killed in his sleep.

"Alive. Guarded. He took breakfast in his chambers. The Dowager is with him." A pause. "He asked to see you this afternoon, my ${title_honorific} Vizier. Specifically you. Not the Council. Not the court."

You eat. The bread is warm, the cheese sharp, the figs sweet enough to make your teeth ache. Through the window, Khazara stretches to the horizon under a sky the color of hammered copper -- domes and minarets catching the sun, the two rivers glittering like veins of silver winding through the body of the city, the Grand Bazaar already humming with the particular music of commerce: haggling voices, the clink of coins, the creak of cart wheels on cobblestones.

The city does not stop. It never stops. Not for embargo, not for assassination, not for the quiet trembling of a boy on a jade throne. Two million souls going about their business of living -- baking bread, selling spice, mending nets, nursing children, tending the sick, arguing with neighbors, falling in love and out again -- trusting, in the unspoken way that citizens trust, that the people in the Citadel above them will keep the world from falling apart.

The thought is staggering. And clarifying.

You pour another cup of cardamom tea and let the steam rise between your hands. The fragrance wraps around you like a warm shawl. You think about what you know. What you have learned. What you have become in these weeks since a nervous secretary met you at the city gates, placed a seal ring on your finger, and said, in his flustered, earnest way, that the empire needed you.

The conspiracy is real. It reaches into the Citadel itself -- into the guard rotations, the intelligence networks, the treasury. Someone with power and resources and patience is systematically weakening the empire from within: inciting the Khanate raids, provoking the trade embargo, murdering your predecessor, and now attempting to kill the Emperor himself. Not random acts. Not separate crises. A coordinated campaign, waged on multiple fronts, with the precision of a military operation and the patience of a spider.

The factions are fracturing under the pressure. Each one sees the crisis through the lens of their own power. Kadir sees a military failure requiring military solutions. Priya sees an economic threat requiring economic answers. Ashoka sees a moral crisis requiring spiritual guidance. Zara sees intelligence failures requiring deeper shadows. The Dowager sees her son in danger and reaches for the only tool she has ever trusted: control.

And the Emperor. The boy on the throne. Sixteen years old, frightened, brave, more intelligent than anyone gives him credit for, asking a question that no one in three hundred years of Khazaran history has been able to answer satisfactorily: *What should I do?*
