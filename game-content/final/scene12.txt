The morning is cold. An unusual wind has come down from the Ashghar Mountains, carrying with it the scent of snow and pine, and you can feel it through the marble walls of your chambers as you dress. The silk of your vizier's robes does nothing against the chill. You fasten the seal ring on your finger and notice that your hands are steady.

They should not be. Not after what Farid told you an hour ago.

A pamphlet is circulating through the bazaar -- cheaply printed, anonymously authored, devastating in its claims. It alleges that Emperor Roshan ordered the execution of a merchant family in the northern provinces to seize their trade routes. The pamphlet names the family. It names the village. It names the children.

The story is a lie. You know this because you have seen the intelligence reports: the family was killed by Khanate raiders, not imperial soldiers. But the lie is crafted with surgical precision, and it has arrived at the worst possible moment -- three days before the Emperor is to address the guilds about trade reform.

*page_break

Farid is waiting in the antechamber, his face the color of old parchment. He has the pamphlet in his hand, and he holds it the way a man holds a dead rat -- at arm's length, with visible distaste.

"My lord Vizier," he says. "The situation is... it is not good. The pamphlet has reached every coffeehouse in the city. The printing press makes things -- well, it makes things fast. The wrong things, mostly."

You take the pamphlet from him. The paper is rough, the ink smudged. Someone printed this in haste, but the words are not hasty. They are deliberate. Every detail is designed to be believable.

"Who wrote this?" you ask.

Farid shakes his head. "Spymaster Zara has her people looking. She believes the conspiracy is behind it. Someone wants the Emperor to look like a tyrant before his address to the guilds."

You set the pamphlet down on your desk. The seal ring catches the morning light, and for a moment you study the jade -- cool, green, ancient. The weight of it has become familiar. You are not sure whether that is reassuring or alarming.

*page_break

The problem is not the pamphlet. Pamphlets can be countered with truth. The problem is the witness.

A man named Jalil, a grain merchant from the northern provinces, has appeared in the bazaar. He claims to have survived the alleged massacre. He weeps in the coffeehouses. He shows scars. He names the soldiers who came to his village. He describes the Emperor's seal on the execution order.

Jalil is lying. You are almost certain of it. The scars are real, but they could have come from the Khanate raid itself. The details he provides about the "execution order" contain errors that anyone familiar with imperial documents would catch -- but the common people of the bazaar are not familiar with imperial documents. They see a weeping man with scars and they believe him.

Afterward, in a quiet room behind the bazaar, you give Tahira the gold Zara promised. She takes it without looking at you. Her daughter stares at you with wide, dark eyes that hold no accusation -- she is too young for that -- but hold a question you cannot answer.
