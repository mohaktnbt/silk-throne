*comment === OPENING: THE DEAD MAN'S DOOR ===

The door to the predecessor's office is on the third floor of the eastern wing, at the end of a corridor where the light thins and the air tastes of dust. You have walked past it twice since arriving at the Citadel. Both times, something made you quicken your step -- a prickling at the nape of your neck, a coldness that had nothing to do with marble and shade.

"Per regulation 15-C," he says, and his voice is too bright, too careful, "the incoming Vizier has full authority to access all records and effects of the predecessor. The key turns with a sound like a bone cracking. The door swings inward on oiled hinges -- someone has maintained those hinges even with the room sealed -- and the smell reaches you first.

Dust. Ink gone dry. Sandalwood incense burned down to memory. Beneath it, something fainter and less pleasant: the sweet, cloying residue of illness. Of a body that suffered before it stopped.

The office is large. A desk of dark walnut dominates the center, still covered in papers as though the man who worked here merely stepped out for tea and never returned. Bookshelves line three walls, floor to ceiling, stuffed with leather-bound volumes and stacked scrolls that lean against one another like drunken soldiers. A window on the far wall overlooks the Garden of Eternal Spring, its shutters drawn, admitting only thin blades of light through the slats.

You can see where he sat. The chair is pushed back at an angle -- the angle of a man who stood too quickly, or who was pulled to his feet. There is a cup on the desk, still in its saucer, the dregs dried to a dark stain at the bottom.

The teacup. You stare at it for a long moment.

*page_break

Farid hovers at the threshold, one foot in the corridor, one in the room, as though his body cannot decide whether loyalty or self-preservation should win.

"He was a good man," Farid says quietly. "Vizier Massoud. He was -- he knew every regulation. Every protocol. He could quote the tax code from memory, all four hundred and twelve articles." A pause. "He was kind to me. He never raised his voice. Even when I spilled coffee on the trade reports."

You watch the way Farid's eyes move across the room -- cataloguing, remembering, grieving. He touches the doorframe the way a man might touch a gravestone. A shaft of dusty light catches his hand, and you notice for the first time that he wears a simple copper band on his smallest finger. A gift, perhaps. From a master who noticed small kindnesses the way he noticed small numbers.

The room seems to hold its breath. Somewhere in the Citadel, a door closes. The sound echoes down the corridor behind you, and Farid startles as though something has touched his shoulder.

"I was home sick the day it happened," he says, and now his voice fractures like glass struck at the wrong angle. "A stomach ailment. Nothing serious. I sent a note. If I had been here --"

"If you had been here, Farid, you might be dead too."

The words land harder than you intend. Farid flinches. But something in his shoulders loosens, as if he has been carrying that thought for weeks and hearing it spoken aloud -- hearing that it might not be his fault -- costs him something enormous and gives him something back.

He nods. Wipes his eyes with the heel of his hand. Adjusts his spectacles. Straightens his robes. The rituals of composure, performed with the desperate precision of a man building a wall out of small habits.
