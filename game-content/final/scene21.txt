*comment === OPENING: THE THREE MESSENGERS ===

The first messenger arrives at dawn.

You are not asleep. You have not slept. The hours since the Eve of Battle have passed in a strange suspended state -- half vigil, half dread -- as you sit in your chambers with the evidence spread before you on the desk like the bones of a dissected animal. The conspiracy laid bare. The true enemy named, if not yet confronted. The empire balanced on a blade's edge, and you the hand that must keep it from falling.

The knock comes at the door. Three sharp raps -- the military cadence.

Farid opens it. A border rider stands in the corridor, dust-caked and swaying on his feet. His horse, you will learn later, died beneath him at the city gates. He rode the last mile on a borrowed mount, the dispatch case clutched to his chest like a child.

"The Northern Khanates," he says. His voice is a ruin. "They've crossed the Wall of Roshan. All of them. Every tribe. Fort Darband has fallen."

*page_break

The words land like stones dropped into your chest.

Fort Darband. The empire's northern shield, the fortress that has held the steppe at bay for a century. Fallen. And not to a raiding party, not to the probing attacks that General Kadir has spent his career repelling. Every tribe. A full invasion, coordinated and overwhelming, striking at the precise moment when the empire's attention is turned inward.

You know, with the cold clarity of exhaustion, that this is no coincidence.

The messenger sags against the doorframe. Farid catches him, guides him to a chair, presses a cup of water into his shaking hands. The man drinks like someone who has forgotten what water tastes like.

"How many?" you ask.

"Thirty thousand horse. Maybe more. They came at night, through the breach in the eastern section -- the section that was due for repair." His eyes are hollow. "Someone told them where the wall was weakest."

*page_break

The second messenger arrives before you have finished reading the dispatch.

This one is not a soldier. She is a trade runner -- one of Priya's network of commercial couriers who carry market intelligence faster than any official dispatch system. She is cleaner than the border rider but no less frightened.

"The Ashghar Pass is closed," she says, without preamble. "The western merchants have seized the customs house. They're demanding renegotiation of all tariff agreements and payment of so-called damages for -- for I don't know what, but the number they're quoting is two hundred thousand gold ashrafs. The caravans are backed up for three miles. And someone has been buying up grain futures in every market between here and the coast."

She pauses, swallows. "Guildmaster Priya says to tell you the treasury has been raided. Not physically -- through falsified withdrawal orders bearing forged seals. She's still calculating the damage, but her first estimate is that a third of the imperial reserve is gone."

A third of the reserve. The number hangs in the air between you like smoke.

*page_break

The third messenger arrives with the sunrise.

He is not a messenger at all. He is Secretary Farid, who has been running between the Citadel's corridors like a man trying to outpace a fire, gathering reports and dispatches and rumors and assembling them into a picture that grows more terrible with each new piece.

He stands in the doorway of your chambers. He is not trembling, which is how you know this is truly bad. When Farid stops trembling, it means the situation has passed beyond the reach of anxiety into something harder and colder.

"Prince Kaveh," he says, "has proclaimed himself the rightful Emperor of the Khazaran Empire."

He holds up a broadsheet -- fresh ink, still damp. You can smell it from across the room. The printing press, that revolutionary engine of ideas and disruption, has been turned to a new purpose. Kaveh's face stares from the paper with the confident half-smile you remember from the Festival of Lights. Beneath it, in bold calligraphy:

*By Right of Blood and Divine Mandate, Prince Kaveh of House Roshan Claims the Silk Throne.*

*page_break

You stand at the window of your chambers and look out over Khazara.

The city is waking. Smoke rises from ten thousand cooking fires. The call to prayer echoes from the Temple Quarter, a sound so familiar it has become part of the air itself. Below the Citadel walls, merchants are opening their stalls in the Grand Bazaar, unaware that the world they knew when they went to sleep has ended.

Three crises. Simultaneous. Coordinated.

An invasion from the north. An economic collapse from within. A political challenge to the throne itself.
