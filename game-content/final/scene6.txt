*comment === OPENING: THE TRADE CRISIS ===

The summons comes at the hour when the evening call to prayer still hangs in the air like smoke.

You are in your chambers, reviewing the day's correspondence by lamplight. The wick needs trimming -- the flame gutters and sways, making the ink on the parchment seem to crawl. Through the open window, the Garden of Eternal Spring exhales the scent of jasmine and wet stone. A fountain murmurs in the courtyard below, its voice as steady and constant as a heartbeat.

It is the kind of evening that could lull you into believing the empire is at peace.

Then Farid bursts through the door without knocking.

He is pale. In the months you have known him, Farid has never once forgotten to knock. He has knocked on doors that were already open. He once knocked on a curtain. Protocol runs through his veins the way the Ashar River runs through the valley -- it simply does not stop.

Tonight, it has stopped.

"My ${title_honorific} Vizier," he says, and the words come out in a rush, tumbling over one another like merchants fleeing a fire. "There is an emergency session of the Council. Guildmaster Priya has called it herself. She invoked the Commercial Crisis Protocol -- regulation 88-A, subsection -- well, the specifics don't matter, though I have the relevant documentation if you -- no. What matters is the Western Confederation has imposed a full trade embargo."

*page_break

The word settles over the room like a stone dropped into still water.

An embargo.

The Western Confederation controls the far end of the Silk Road. Every bolt of Khazaran silk, every sack of pepper, every chest of rubies that crosses the Ashghar Mountains passes through their ports and markets before reaching the wider world. They are the gateway, and tonight they have slammed it shut.

If the Confederation closes its doors to Khazaran goods, the empire does not simply lose profit. It hemorrhages. The tariff revenue that funds the army, maintains the roads, feeds the garrisons on the northern wall -- all of it flows from trade. Cut the trade, and the empire begins to starve. Not in months. In weeks.

You rise and reach for your formal robes. Farid is already holding them out, his hands trembling slightly. You notice he has brought the heavier robe -- the one with gold thread at the collar and the imperial seal embroidered on the breast. He wants you to look imposing. You are grateful for the instinct.

"How bad?" you ask, fastening the sash.

"Guildmaster Priya used the word 'catastrophic,'" Farid says, following you into the corridor. His sandals slap against the marble in a frantic rhythm. "She does not use that word lightly. She once described a warehouse fire that destroyed ten thousand gold dirhams' worth of silk as 'an inconvenience.' She described the collapse of the southern bridge -- the one that killed three mules and blocked traffic for a week -- as 'suboptimal.'"

"And this is catastrophic."

"This is catastrophic."

The corridors of the Citadel are never fully dark. Oil lamps burn in iron sconces every twenty paces, casting pools of warm amber light between stretches of shadow. You pass a pair of guards who snap to attention as you approach, their armor catching the lamplight in brief, sharp flashes. The air smells of sandalwood incense and the faint, ever-present musk of old stone. The Citadel is three centuries old, and no amount of perfume entirely masks its age.

*page_break

The Council Chamber is lit by a hundred oil lamps, their flames reflected in the polished surface of the round table. The air smells of frankincense and anxiety -- the latter you identify by the particular quality of the silence when you enter. It is the silence of people who have been arguing and have paused to watch the door.

Everyone is already present. A rare thing.

General Kadir stands by the window with his iron hand resting on the sill, his jaw clenched so tight you can see the muscle jumping in his cheek. The prosthetic gleams dully in the lamplight -- dark iron, articulated at the wrist, a thing of grim utility. His good hand rests on the pommel of his sword, though there is nothing in this room to fight. The habit of a lifetime spent at war.

High Priest Ashoka sits in his customary stillness, hands folded in his lap, eyes half-closed in an expression that might be meditation or might be a man calculating three moves ahead while appearing to pray. His white robes are immaculate. They are always immaculate. You have begun to suspect that this is, itself, a form of political statement.

Spymaster Zara occupies her chair in the deepest shadow, as always. She has chosen the seat where the lamplight does not quite reach, where her face is a study in angles and darkness. Her expression is unreadable. It is always unreadable. You have begun to suspect that the expression itself is the message: *I see everything. You see nothing of me.*

And Guildmaster Priya is pacing.

You have never seen Priya pace. Priya sits. Priya lounges. Priya leans back in her chair with a cup of wine and presides over conversations with the easy confidence of a woman who knows the price of everything in the room, including the people. She smiles when others frown and laughs when others shout. Pacing is not in her vocabulary.

Tonight, it is.

She stops when you enter. Her dark eyes fix on you with an intensity that cuts through the lamplight like a blade through gauze.

"Vizier. Thank you for coming quickly." Her voice is controlled -- Priya's voice is always controlled -- but beneath the control you hear something you have never heard from her before: fear. "Sit down. I have numbers, and they are ugly."

You take your seat at the round table. The seal ring on your finger catches the lamplight -- jade and silver, a small cold weight that carries the authority of three centuries. Every person at this table is watching you. You can feel it the way you feel the heat of a fire: a pressure on the skin, invisible but unmistakable.

*page_break

Priya does not sit. She stands at the head of the table and speaks as if presenting testimony before a court.

"Six hours ago, the Confederation Merchant Council voted unanimously to halt all trade with the Khazaran Empire. Effective immediately. Every Khazaran merchant ship currently in Confederation ports is impounded. Every caravan approaching the western passes is being turned back. The Ashghar Pass customs house -- the largest land crossing between our empire and the western world -- has been shuttered."

She lets the silence do its work. She is good at this -- the merchant's instinct for timing, for knowing when a pause is worth more than words.

"The cost," she continues, her voice dropping half a register, "is forty thousand gold dirhams per week. Per *week*. That is not an abstract number, Vizier. That is soldiers who do not get paid. Roads that do not get built. Medicines that do not reach frontier garrisons. A grandmother in the provinces who does not eat." She places both hands flat on the table, fingers spread, as if she is physically holding the empire's economy together by force of will. "This is not a diplomatic spat. This is an attack on the empire's lifeblood. Every day we spend arguing about a response is a day we bleed."

General Kadir breaks the silence with a fist on the windowsill. The sound is startling in the enclosed chamber -- iron on stone, a battlefield sound in a room built for whispers.

"Then we respond in kind. Send the navy. Blockade their southern ports. See how they like it when their own merchants come begging at our gates."

"With what navy?" Priya snaps, turning on him. "We have thirty warships. They have a hundred and fifty. We are a land power, General. Naval threats from us are a joke they tell in Confederation taverns while drinking wine from goblets they bought with our tariff money."

"Then send the army through the passes. Seize the customs house by force."

"And start a war that will destroy the very trade routes we are trying to protect. Brilliant. That is like burning down the bazaar to kill a rat."

*page_break

High Priest Ashoka opens his eyes. When he speaks, his voice is the calm at the center of the storm -- low, measured, each word placed with the precision of a master calligrapher's brushstroke.

"The Flame teaches us that the hand which strikes in anger often wounds itself. Perhaps before we discuss responses, we should first understand causes. Why has the Confederation acted now? What specific provocation has produced this drastic response?"

All eyes turn to Spymaster Zara.

She is quiet for a moment. The deliberate pause -- you have come to recognize it as the interval in which Zara selects exactly which truths to share and which to hold in reserve. She is always choosing. Her entire existence is an exercise in selective disclosure.

"A Khazaran intelligence operative was captured in the Confederation port of Westmarch three weeks ago," she says at last. Her voice is flat, factual, stripped of inflection. "He was carrying documents related to Confederation naval deployments. Fleet movements. Harbor defense rotations. Shipyard production schedules. The Confederation considers this an act of espionage."

The room erupts. Kadir demands to know who authorized the operation. Priya wants to know why she was not informed of an intelligence action that could trigger an economic crisis. Ashoka speaks of the wisdom of transparency and the dangers of shadow operations conducted without the knowledge of the Flame. Their voices overlap and collide -- General's bark, Guildmaster's snap, Priest's measured reproach -- filling the chamber with the particular cacophony of powerful people discovering they have been blindsided.

Zara endures the storm without expression. She sits in her shadow and waits. She is very good at waiting.

"I did not authorize the operation," she says, when the noise subsides. Her voice has not changed -- still flat, still factual. "The operative was not mine."

The silence that follows is different from the one before. This one has teeth.

"If he was not yours," you say into that silence, "then whose was he?"

Zara's eyes meet yours across the table. In the lamplight, they are dark and sharp and absolutely unforgiving.

"That," she says, "is an excellent question."

*comment === MEETING WITH AMBASSADOR CHEN ===

*label prepare_chen_meeting

*page_break

Regardless of your strategy, one thing is clear: you must speak with Ambassador Chen. Whether to negotiate, to deliver an ultimatum, to extract information, or to stall for time, the Confederation's representative in Khazara is the fulcrum on which this crisis turns. He is the face of the embargo, and faces can be read.

You send word to the Foreign Quarter. The response comes within minutes -- a polite note on cream-colored paper, sealed with the Confederation's eagle-and-compass emblem. Chen, it seems, has been waiting for your message. You suspect the note was written before your messenger arrived.

The meeting is arranged in a neutral space: a private room in the House of the Blue Fountain, a diplomatic guesthouse near the Foreign Quarter. The room is small but elegant -- turquoise tiles on the walls in geometric patterns that catch and scatter the lamplight, a low table of dark wood between two cushioned seats, and a fountain in the corner whose gentle splashing masks the conversation from any listeners beyond the door.

The fountain is not decorative. It is strategic. In Khazara, every beautiful thing serves at least two purposes.

*page_break

Ambassador Chen arrives precisely on time. He is a lean man of middle years, clean-shaven, with the sharp, watchful eyes of a merchant assessing goods. He is impeccably dressed in Confederation fashion -- tailored jacket, embroidered vest, high polished boots -- but with a Khazaran silk sash draped over one shoulder. A deliberate blend of cultures, a visual argument for the interconnectedness he is currently working to sever.

His Khazari is excellent, spoken with a slight accent that you suspect he maintains by choice rather than necessity. The accent says: *I am foreign. I am not bound by your rules. I can say things your people cannot.*

"Vizier," he says, offering a bow that is correct to the millimeter -- respectful without being deferential, formal without being cold. "Thank you for seeing me so promptly. I had hoped we might discuss recent developments over civilized refreshment."

He gestures to the table, where a servant has laid out cardamom tea and almond pastries on porcelain dishes that are, you note, of Khazaran manufacture. Another message: *We value your products. We simply wish to renegotiate the terms.*

"I took the liberty," he adds, with a smile that reveals nothing and suggests everything.

*page_break

You study him as you take your seat. His face is composed, pleasant, as smooth and unreadable as the porcelain on the table. He might be greeting a friend for afternoon tea. He might be negotiating the fate of empires.

He is doing both.

"Ambassador," you say. "The Confederation has imposed a full trade embargo on the Khazaran Empire. I trust you can explain why."

Chen pours tea with the careful precision of a man who has made an art of the gesture. The liquid is pale gold, fragrant with cardamom. Steam curls between you, rising and dissipating in the lamplight like a thought not quite formed.

"The Confederation Merchant Council acted in response to a specific provocation," he says, setting the teapot down with the exact same control. "A Khazaran operative was apprehended in Westmarch carrying classified documents. Naval deployments, harbor defense rotations, fleet movement schedules." He aligns the teapot with the edge of the tray. "You can appreciate that this was... alarming."

"The operative was not sanctioned by the Khazaran intelligence service."

"So I am told." Chen's smile does not waver. "But he carried Khazaran documents. He spoke Khazari. He was funded with Khazaran gold ashrafs. The Confederation must act on what it sees, not on what it is told."

The fountain murmurs between you, filling the silence with its silver commentary. You lift the tea to your lips. It is perfectly brewed -- the cardamom precise, the sweetness restrained, the temperature ideal. Even Chen's tea is diplomatic.
