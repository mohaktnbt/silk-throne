*comment === OPENING: THE COUNTING HOUSE ===

The imperial treasury smells of dust, cold stone, and fear.

You descend into the vaulted chambers beneath the Citadel where the empire's wealth is stored -- or was stored, before the conspiracy's economic weapons found their mark. The Treasury Hall is a long, low room with iron-banded doors and walls three feet thick, built by Emperor Roshan II specifically to resist siege, riot, and the particular variety of greed that afflicts governments in crisis. Oil lamps in iron brackets cast hard, unforgiving shadows. Every surface here is designed for security, not beauty. No marble. No gold leaf. No geometric tilework in blue and turquoise. Just stone and iron and counting tables and the weight of the empire's lifeblood -- measured in ashrafs and dirhams, recorded in ledgers, stored in chests that should be full and are not.

The silence is wrong. The treasury is never silent. On an ordinary day, this chamber hums with the sounds of commerce -- the scratch of pens, the clink of coins being counted, the murmur of clerks conducting the endless arithmetic that keeps the empire running. Today the silence has the quality of a room after a funeral. The counting tables are empty. The clerks have been dismissed. The only sound is the scratch of a single pen, rapid and relentless, coming from the far end of the hall.

She looks like what she was before she was Guildmaster: a merchant's daughter, working through the night by lamplight because the numbers will not wait for morning.

She has been here for days. You can see it in the lines around her mouth, the hollows beneath her eyes, the slight tremor in her writing hand that she compensates for by pressing harder against the paper. The ledger beneath her pen is covered in figures, annotations, cross-references, each one a thread in the web of the empire's finances, and the web is unraveling.

*comment === FIRST REAL CHOICE: ECONOMIC STRATEGY ===

Priya spreads three documents on the counting table, each one written in her precise hand, each one representing a different path through the economic crisis. She treats them like merchandise -- laying them out, stepping back, letting the customer examine the goods before the negotiation begins.

"Option one: Emergency taxation. We impose a one-time crisis levy on every household in the empire, scaled by assessed wealth. Rich households pay more. Poor households pay less. It raises approximately eighty thousand ashrafs within two weeks -- enough to refill the treasury to functional levels and ride out the crisis. The cost: political fury from every direction. The nobility will call it confiscation. The merchants will call it extortion. The common people, who pay the least in absolute terms but feel the pinch most keenly, will call it theft. No one likes a new tax, Vizier. Especially in a crisis."

She moves to the second document.

"Option two: Foreign loans. I have contacts with the Eastern Trading Houses across the Jade Sea -- old relationships, built on years of commercial cooperation. They will lend us one hundred thousand ashrafs at twelve percent annual interest, secured against future tariff revenues from the eastern ports. It fills the treasury immediately with no domestic political cost. The cost: the empire's financial sovereignty. For the first time in three centuries, the Khazaran Empire will be in debt to foreign bankers. If we default, the Eastern Houses will have legal claim to our tariff system. We become a debtor empire, and debtor empires do not choose their own destinies."

The third document.

"Option three: Market intervention. Instead of raising money, we attack the grain speculation directly. I have identified the five major speculators. At least three are connected to the conspiracy. We seize their assets, break the speculation ring, and use the confiscated grain to stabilize bread prices. It does not refill the treasury, but it prevents the riots that could destroy the city. The cost: we set a precedent for government seizure of private property. Every merchant in the empire will see the government reaching into private warehouses and wonder: am I next?"
