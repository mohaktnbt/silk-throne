The economic strategy is in motion. But strategy addresses the macro crisis. The micro crisis -- the human cost -- demands its own reckoning.

Priya takes you to the window of the treasury office. Below, in the courtyard, a line of petitioners stretches from the administrative gate to the outer wall. Hundreds of people, waiting in the autumn cold for an audience with someone who can help them.

"The war has disrupted supply lines. Food prices have doubled. Rents are rising because refugees from the northern provinces are flooding the city." Priya's voice is quiet -- the tone she uses when the numbers represent people rather than abstractions. "The wealthy are uncomfortable. The middle class is struggling. The poor are starving."

She turns to you.

"We have limited relief funds. Enough to help some, but not all. The question is: who do we protect?"

The Grand Bazaar is visible from the corridor window -- its vaulted roofs stretching across the city's commercial heart. Even now, even in crisis, the bazaar is alive. Merchants haggle. Goods change hands. The machinery of commerce turns, because commerce is what keeps two million people fed and housed and connected to each other.

Priya understood that. She has always understood it. The treasury war was not about numbers in a ledger. It was about the web of transactions -- the buying and selling and lending and borrowing -- that holds a civilization together. When the web tears, the civilization falls. Not with a dramatic crash, but with the quiet, grinding misery of people who can no longer afford bread.
