The ride back to Khazara takes two days. The steppe gives way to foothills, the foothills to the fertile valley, and with each mile southward the landscape grows softer, greener, more cultivated -- and the memory of the crumbling Wall grows heavier. You carry Navid's journal, the forged requisitions, and a weight in your chest that was not there when you left. The northern border is not an abstraction anymore. It is real -- the smell of horse and dust, the sound of wind over the steppe, the faces of soldiers who serve and suffer while the capital debates tariffs and precedence.

Kadir rides beside you in silence for the first hour. The steppe wind is at your back, pushing you south. A hawk circles overhead, its shadow skimming the grass like a dark thought.

Then Kadir speaks.

"The boy emperor. Roshan." He says the name like a soldier says the name of a commander he has not yet decided to follow. Testing the weight of it. Measuring its quality. "Is he worth it?"

"Yes."

"That was fast."

"Some questions do not need deliberation, General."

Kadir grunts. It might be approval. With him, it is difficult to tell. He rides a few more lengths in silence, then speaks again, his voice lower.

"His father was a good emperor. Darius knew the border. He rode north every spring, inspected the garrisons, spoke to the soldiers by name. When he made a decision about troop deployment, it was because he had stood on the ground and tasted the dust and looked into the eyes of the men who would carry out his orders." Kadir pauses. "The boy has never been north. Until today, neither had his Vizier."

"And now?"

"And now you have stood on the ground. You have tasted the dust. You have seen the Wall. The question is what you do with that knowledge."

"I use it."

Kadir turns in his saddle to look at you. The wind catches his cloak, and for a moment he looks like one of the old bronze statues in the Citadel's Hall of Generals -- a warrior frozen in the act of assessment, his iron hand resting on his saddle horn like a weapon at rest.

"Good," he says. And rides on.

*page_break

On the second day, you stop at a village to water the horses. It is a small place -- thirty houses, a well, a granary, a shrine to the Eternal Flame consisting of a brass lamp on a stone pedestal. The villagers come out to watch the cavalry pass with the wary curiosity of people who live on the edge of danger. Children peer from behind their mothers' skirts. An old farmer approaches and offers water with trembling hands.

"The soldiers," he says, looking at Kadir's escort. "Are they going north?"

"Yes."

"Good. The raiders came last month. They took three goats and a cow. We have nothing else for them to take." He pauses. "We also lost a boy. Fourteen. He tried to stop them." The old farmer's voice does not break. It simply empties, as if the grief has been wrung from it by repetition.

Kadir listens. His face is stone. When the farmer finishes, the General dismounts, crouches before the man, and takes both his hands in his own -- the flesh and the iron.

"His name," Kadir says.

"Arash. His name was Arash."

"I will remember it."

The moment passes. You remount and ride on. But the name stays with you, carried south alongside Navid's journal and the forged requisitions. Arash. Fourteen years old. Dead because a wall was crumbling and a supply convoy was redirected by a forged document.

The capital appears on the horizon on the second day -- the domes and minarets rising from the valley like a crown of gold and white. It is beautiful. It is also, you now understand, a city built on a border that is being deliberately eroded. If the Wall falls, if the Khanates pour through in force, this beauty will burn.

You will not let that happen. The Emperor has made his first stand. General Kadir has pledged his support. Navid watches the shadows. The alliance is fragile, held together by trust that has not yet been tested.

But it is a beginning. And beginnings are enough.
