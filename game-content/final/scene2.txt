*comment === OPENING: MORNING PREPARATION ===

Dawn arrives the way power arrives: slowly at first, then all at once.

The first sound is the call to prayer. It rises from the Temple Quarter in a single voice -- a muezzin on the highest minaret, his words climbing the scale in a melody that predates the empire itself. Then a second voice joins, from a minaret further away. Then a third, a fourth, a dozen, until the entire city is singing and the air vibrates with a harmony so vast it seems to come from the sky itself. The sound enters your room through the latticed shutters and settles on your skin like mist.

You are already awake. You have been awake for an hour, lying in the unfamiliar bed, listening to the fountains and rehearsing. The audience. The Emperor. The Dowager. Every word, every gesture, every angle of a bow -- all of it scripted by three centuries of court protocol, and all of it, Farid has assured you, capable of ending a career if performed incorrectly.

A knock. Farid enters carrying your formal robes over one arm and a stack of documents under the other. He looks as though he has not slept at all. There are ink smudges on his left cheek and a wild light in his eyes that you are learning to recognize as focused panic.

"Good morning, ${title_honorific} ${player_name}. The audience is in ninety minutes. I have prepared annotated notes on court protocol -- " he lays papers on the desk " -- a summary of the Emperor's known preferences and habits -- " more papers " -- a brief history of previous Vizier investitures for context -- " yet more papers " -- and a list of seventeen things you absolutely must not do in the Jade Throne Room."

He pauses, slightly out of breath.

"The list was originally twenty-three items, but I consolidated the six involving unauthorized touching of the throne into a single entry."

The formal robes of the Grand Vizier are magnificent and impractical in equal measure. Layers of silk in deep indigo, embroidered with silver thread in geometric patterns that catch the light. "The key points," he says, as he works. "First: you will enter the Jade Throne Room from the Hall of Mirrors. You will walk the full length of the chamber alone. The court will be assembled on either side. They will be watching you. Do not hurry. Do not hesitate. Walk as though you belong there."

The shadow of the dead Vizier falls across the morning like a stain.

*comment === THE WALK TO THE THRONE ROOM ===

*comment === THE JADE THRONE ROOM ===

The doors open, and the Jade Throne Room takes your breath.

You have seen it before -- briefly, yesterday, in the amber light of late afternoon, with only Farid and an attendant for company. But that was the room at rest. This is the room at full ceremony, and the difference is the difference between a sword on a wall and a sword in a hand.

The chamber is vast. The domed ceiling rises a hundred feet above you, painted with a field of stars on midnight blue, each star picked out in gold leaf that catches the light from the high arched windows. The floor is polished marble inlaid with geometric patterns in gold and lapis lazuli. Columns of white marble line the hall in two rows, and between them, the court is assembled.

They stand in ranked rows, perhaps two hundred of them: nobles in silk and brocade, military officers in crimson and gold, temple officials in white, merchants in the rich, practical fabrics of the trading class. Every face is turned toward you. Every eye is measuring.

The silence is extraordinary. Two hundred people, and the only sound is the distant splash of a fountain, the faint crackle of incense burners, and the soft tread of your own footsteps on the marble floor. Each step carries you forward and simultaneously backward -- back through three centuries of Viziers who have made this walk, some to glory, some to ruin, all of them swallowed eventually by the same marble and silence and history that surrounds you now.

The walls are decorated with panels of calligraphy -- verses from the scripture of the Eternal Flame, written in a hand so fine and so large that the letters seem to float on the stone. You recognize one passage: "By wisdom is the throne established, and by fire is it defended." The gold ink catches the sunlight, and the words seem to pulse with a light of their own.

The incense is sandalwood -- heavy, warm, sweet, settling on your tongue and in your lungs. The braziers are placed at intervals along the colonnade, each one breathing a thin plume of smoke that rises straight up in the still air before spreading into a translucent haze beneath the dome. The entire room smells of time. Of power. Of the slow accumulation of three hundred years of ceremony and blood and ambition, layered into the stone like geological strata.

At the far end, on the dais of white marble, sits the Silk Throne.

And on the Silk Throne sits a boy.

*page_break

Emperor Roshan IV is trying very hard not to look afraid.

He sits upright, shoulders squared, chin lifted at the precise angle that portraits of his ancestors depict. His robes are white -- pure, unblemished white, the color reserved exclusively for the Emperor -- and they are too large for him. The sleeves have been taken in, but the collar still swallows his neck. His hands rest on the jade arms of the throne, and if you did not know better, you might think the posture was relaxed. But his knuckles are pale, and his fingers grip the jade as if he fears the throne might buck like a horse and throw him.

He is sixteen years old. He has a sharp, intelligent face -- high cheekbones, a strong jaw that has not yet fully filled out, dark eyes that are watching you with ferocious attention. There is the barest shadow of a beard on his chin, carefully trimmed, and you suspect he checks it in the mirror every morning with the anxious hope of a boy willing whiskers into existence. The crown sits on his dark hair -- a circlet of gold set with a single emerald the size of a quail's egg that catches the light from the high windows and sends a green flash across the marble floor whenever he turns his head.

His hands fascinate you. They are a boy's hands -- smooth, unmarked, the nails clean and filed. They have never held a sword in anger or signed a death warrant or gripped the arm of a throne while an empire balanced on the decisions made from that seat. Yet here they are, resting on the jade arms of the Silk Throne with a forced stillness that speaks of countless hours of coaching: sit like this, hold your hands like this, do not fidget, do not scratch, do not let them see you tremble.

He is, you realize with a pang, the same age as your youngest cousin. The same age as the gate guards you trained in the provinces. A child, wearing the heaviest crown in the world.

You notice other details. A cup of water on a small table beside the throne -- untouched, but placed within reach, the way a mother places a glass beside a child's bed. A folded cloth on the throne's arm -- white silk, for wiping the sweat that the heat and the heavy robes must produce. These are small kindnesses, invisible to anyone not looking for them, and they tell you that someone in this palace cares about the boy on the throne, even if that care is expressed in the language of protocol rather than affection.

Beside the throne, seated in a chair of carved ebony that is exactly two inches lower than the throne itself -- a difference that someone, at some point, measured with obsessive precision -- is the Dowager Empress Nur.

*page_break

The Dowager does not look like a woman who measures things in inches. She looks like a woman who measures things in years.

She is forty-five, though she could be any age between thirty and sixty. Her face is smooth, composed, framed by a headscarf of midnight blue embroidered with silver stars. Her robes are dark -- deep blue, almost black -- and unadorned except for a single brooch of diamonds and sapphires at her throat. She wears no other jewelry. She does not need to.

Her eyes are the most remarkable thing about her. Dark, steady, and utterly without warmth. They rest on you as you approach, and you have the sudden, unsettling sense that she has already finished reading you -- that she knew everything she needed to know the moment the doors opened, and the rest of this ceremony is merely a formality she endures out of respect for tradition.

She does not smile. She does not frown. She simply watches, with the patient attention of someone who has been watching for a very long time.

You walk the length of the Jade Throne Room. It takes an eternity. You keep your pace even, your eyes forward, your shoulders level. The court watches. The Dowager watches. The boy on the throne watches, and in his eyes, behind the studied formality, you see something raw and desperate: hope.

He hopes you are the answer to a question he has not yet learned to ask.

Three steps from the dais, you stop. You bow.

One heartbeat. The marble floor, polished to a mirror finish, reflects your face back at you -- upside down, compressed, a stranger.

Two heartbeats. You hear the court breathing. Two hundred people, holding their breath, waiting for the new Vizier to rise.

Three heartbeats. Not two. Not four. Three, as protocol demands, and not a fraction of a second longer. You straighten.

The Emperor is looking at you. The Dowager is looking at you. The entire court is looking at you. And in the space between the bow and the first word, in that hanging silence where the future has not yet been spoken, you feel the full weight of the moment: the history of this room, the power of the throne, the fragility of the boy who sits on it, and the ring on your finger that says you belong here, whether you believe it or not.
