Dawn breaks over Khazara like a bruise -- purple and gray and wrong. The call to prayer rises from the Temple Quarter, its notes distorted by the autumn wind, bending around corners and through alleyways until the sound that reaches your window is barely recognizable as music. It sounds like mourning. The call to prayer echoes across the city as it does every morning, but today the sound seems thin, stretched, a voice calling into a void where no answer waits.

You have not slept. You sit at your desk in the clothes you wore to the Festival, the crimson silk creased, the gold thread catching the first gray light from the window. The seal ring sits on your finger like a shackle. The reports before you blur. You have read the same line seven times.

The words are Kadir's -- blunt, precise, devastating. He has formally accused the Grand Vizier of incompetence in matters of imperial defense. The documents are forgeries. You know this because you did not authorize those transfers. But the forgeries are masterful -- your seal, your signature, the specific bureaucratic language that only someone with intimate knowledge of the Treasury's procedures could replicate.

By evening, every tea house, every market stall, every household in Khazara will know that the High Priest -- the voice of the gods, the keeper of the sacred fire -- has named the Grand Vizier as the source of the empire's ills.

Ashoka, who spoke in parables and called you "child." "The temple guards have been mobilized," Farid says. "They are -- they are not threatening anyone, my lord Vizier. They are simply... present. In the streets. Near the Citadel. Two thousand armed men, praying."

The distinction between praying soldiers and threatening soldiers is, you realize, entirely academic.
