# The Silk Throne -- Style Guide

Prose and technical rules for all writers. Every scene file must conform to these standards. When in doubt, consult this document. Consistency is the goal.

---

## PROSE STYLE

### Tone and Voice

The prose style is **rich, literary, and immersive** -- Hilary Mantel's political intimacy meets George R.R. Martin's world-building scope, filtered through the sensory vividness of a Mughal court. The writing should feel elevated without being purple. Every sentence should earn its place.

**DO:**
- Use vivid, specific sensory details (the smell of sandalwood incense, the weight of silk robes, the taste of cardamom tea)
- Write with precision -- the right word, not the almost-right word
- Let silence and space do work (what a character does NOT say matters)
- Create atmosphere through concrete details rather than abstract description
- Ground political intrigue in physical reality (sweat, clenched fists, averted eyes)

**DO NOT:**
- Write generic fantasy prose ("the great hall was vast and impressive")
- Over-explain emotions ("she felt angry, which was evident from her expression")
- Use modern slang or anachronisms
- Break the fourth wall or address the reader as audience
- Use excessive exclamation marks (one per 5,000 words maximum)

### Point of View and Tense

**Second person, present tense.** This is mandatory for ChoiceScript games.

```
You step into the Jade Throne Room. The air smells of sandalwood
and old stone. Sunlight falls through the high windows in long
shafts, catching the gold thread in the courtiers' robes. At the
far end, on a dais of white marble, sits the Silk Throne -- carved
from a single block of jade so pale it seems to glow.

The boy on it is trying very hard not to look afraid.
```

### Paragraph Length

Short paragraphs. This is read on screens, often mobile. Follow these rules:

- **Standard paragraph:** 2-4 sentences
- **Maximum paragraph:** 5 sentences (rare, for important moments)
- **Action paragraphs:** 1-2 sentences (quick, punchy)
- **Dialogue paragraphs:** 1 line of dialogue + 1-2 lines of action/description

Never write a paragraph longer than 5 sentences. If the content requires more, break it into two paragraphs.

### Sensory Palette

Writers should draw from these sensory details consistently to create the Khazaran atmosphere:

**Sights:**
- White marble, gold leaf, geometric tilework in blue and turquoise
- Silk in crimson, saffron, and midnight blue
- Fountains catching light, gardens in geometric precision
- Candlelight and oil lamps casting warm shadows
- The Eternal Flame's glow reflected on stone walls
- Dust motes in shafts of sunlight

**Sounds:**
- Water: fountains, channels, rain on marble
- Music: the tar (lute), daf (drum), ney (flute)
- Voices: murmured conversations, the call to prayer, market haggling
- Silence: the absence of sound in the Throne Room is deafening

**Smells:**
- Incense: sandalwood, frankincense, oud
- Food: cardamom, saffron, rosewater, roasting lamb
- Flowers: jasmine, rose, orange blossom
- Other: old paper, horse leather, steel, river water

**Tastes:**
- Spiced wine (warm, cinnamon-laced)
- Cardamom tea (sweet, fragrant)
- Pomegranate (tart, bright)
- Saffron rice (warm, rich)
- Rosewater sherbet (cold, sweet)

**Textures:**
- Cool marble underfoot
- Heavy silk on the shoulders
- The Vizier's seal ring (smooth jade, metal band)
- Rough parchment, smooth vellum
- The grip of a sword hilt
- Warm bread torn by hand

### Show, Don't Tell

Describe emotions through physical manifestation, not labels.

**WRONG:**
```
The Dowager is angry.
```

**RIGHT:**
```
The Dowager's fingers tighten on the arm of her chair. Her smile
does not change. If anything, it grows wider. That is how you
know she is furious.
```

**WRONG:**
```
You feel nervous about the meeting.
```

**RIGHT:**
```
Your mouth is dry. The seal ring feels heavier than usual on your
finger. You straighten your robes for the third time and force
yourself to stop.
```

### Pacing

Alternate between scene types to create rhythm:

1. **High tension** (confrontations, battles, revelations) -- short paragraphs, fast pacing, frequent page breaks
2. **Medium tension** (political negotiations, investigations) -- standard pacing, mix of dialogue and description
3. **Low tension** (hub scenes, romance, quiet moments) -- longer descriptions, slower pacing, sensory richness
4. **Transition** (travel, preparation) -- brief, functional, move the story forward

The general rhythm within a scene:

```
[Quiet opening -- set the scene, 200 words]
[Rising tension -- the situation develops, 300 words]
[*page_break]
[CHOICE or confrontation -- the moment of decision]
[Consequence -- immediate fallout, 200 words]
[*page_break]
[New tension or quiet aftermath, 300 words]
[Repeat cycle 2-3 more times per scene]
```

## CHARACTER VOICES

Every named NPC must sound distinct. Readers should be able to identify the speaker from dialogue alone, without attribution. Below are the voice rules and three example lines for each major character.

### Emperor Roshan (16, trying to be regal)

**Voice rules:**
- Formal register in public (royal "we," elaborate phrasing)
- More natural in private (catches himself, corrects to formal)
- Historical references ("Roshan the First would have...")
- Short, emotional bursts when upset (youth showing through)
- Never uses slang or casual language

**Examples:**
1. "We have considered the counsel of our advisors and find it... wanting. The empire requires action, not deliberation. We will have proposals by morning. That is not a request."
2. "Do you think my father ever felt this way? Like the crown is too heavy and the room is too full of people who know more than he does? He never looked like that. He always looked like he knew."
3. "I am sixteen. I know that. Everyone in this room knows that. But Roshan the First was seventeen when he conquered the Valley. Age is not wisdom, but neither is it ignorance."

### Dowager Empress Nur (45, imperial chess master)

**Voice rules:**
- Always speaks in metaphors and parables
- Never raises her voice
- Uses "we" even in private
- Calls the player "child" (condescending), "Vizier" (formal), or their name (rare sincerity)
- Her compliments are often veiled threats, and vice versa
- Long, flowing sentences that circle their point

**Examples:**
1. "The river does not argue with the stone. It goes around, or it wears the stone down, or it waits for the rain. We have always admired the river's patience. You should too, child."
2. "We chose you, Vizier, because an empty vessel can be filled with whatever is needed. Pray do not make us regret discovering that the vessel has a crack."
3. "My son will be a great emperor. This is not a hope. It is a certainty. We have spent twenty years ensuring it. If you share this certainty, we shall be the firmest of allies. If you do not... well. The river is patient."

### General Kadir (55, blunt soldier)

**Voice rules:**
- Short sentences. Military brevity.
- Military metaphors ("flanking," "holding the line," "supply chain")
- Rarely uses anyone's first name -- rank or title
- Gets quieter when angry, not louder
- Occasional dark humor, always dry
- Physical punctuation (pounds table, points at maps)

**Examples:**
1. "Fifty dead. Fort Darband's outer wall breached. The Khanates are coordinating. Someone is feeding them our rotation schedules. I need troops. I need gold. I need this council to stop arguing about tariffs and start arguing about survival."
2. "Vizier. With respect, and I mean that in the military sense where it means none at all, you have never held a sword in anger. Don't tell me how to fight. Tell me what you want dead and get out of my way."
3. "I've buried better soldiers than anyone in this room will ever be. That's not a boast. That's the cost of command. You want to know if I'm loyal? I'm loyal to the soldiers I haven't buried yet."

### Guildmaster Priya (38, charming dealmaker)

**Voice rules:**
- Warm, conversational, disarmingly friendly
- Financial and commercial metaphors ("invest," "returns," "compound interest")
- Laughs easily (but her eyes are always calculating)
- Code-switches between formal court speech and earthy merchant talk
- Often talks over food and drink
- Occasionally drops the mask to show steel

**Examples:**
1. "The embargo is costing us forty thousand gold dirhams per week. That's not an abstract number, Vizier. That's soldiers who don't get paid. That's a road that doesn't get built. That's a grandmother in the provinces who doesn't eat. Money is never abstract."
2. "Have some wine. It's from the southern provinces -- lovely vintage, cost me too much, but quality is an investment. Now, about this alliance you're proposing. What's my return?"
3. "People make the mistake of thinking I'm soft because I offer them tea and smile. My father sold pepper from a wooden cart. I built a trading empire before I was thirty. Don't confuse my manners for weakness."

### High Priest Ashoka (62, serene authority)

**Voice rules:**
- Calm, measured, unhurried
- Speaks in parables and scripture
- Uses rhetorical questions as weapons
- Addresses people as "child" regardless of age or rank (except the Emperor)
- Drops into a resonant, formal register for pronouncements
- Long pauses before speaking

**Examples:**
1. "The Fourth Wisdom teaches that temperance is not the absence of desire but the mastery of it. You desire power, child. That is natural. The question the Flame asks is: does your power serve the people, or do the people serve your power?"
2. "I have heard confessions in this temple for forty years. Generals have wept before me. Merchants have begged forgiveness. Empresses have whispered secrets that would burn the empire to ash. Do you imagine I am shocked by your revelations? I am merely... disappointed."
3. "The Flame does not take sides. The Flame illuminates. It shows what is, not what we wish to see. If you stand in its light, child, be prepared to see yourself as you truly are."

### Spymaster Zara (33, precise and dangerous)

**Voice rules:**
- Sparse. Economical. Never uses three words when one will do.
- Dry, dark humor (unexpected, catches people off guard)
- States facts without emotional coloring
- Rarely uses titles -- just names, or nothing
- Pauses before speaking (choosing each word carefully)
- When she wants to be persuasive, she simply presents evidence

**Examples:**
1. "Poisoned. Blue lotus venom. Three to five days. Your predecessor was dead before anyone knew he was dying. I've narrowed it to five suspects. Would you like the list?"
2. "Trust. That's a word people use when they want something for free. I don't deal in trust. I deal in evidence and mutual interest. Show me one of those and we can work together."
3. "You're being followed. Two men, since the bazaar. They're good but I'm better. Walk normally. Turn left at the fountain. I'll handle it. Don't look back."

### Secretary Farid (25, anxious and loyal)

**Voice rules:**
- Flustered, over-formal, tends to ramble
- Stammers when nervous (represented by repeated words and self-corrections)
- Protocol citations ("Per regulation 47-B...")
- "My lord/lady Vizier" constantly
- Unexpectedly eloquent about administrative systems
- Unintentionally funny

**Examples:**
1. "The -- the Council meeting is in twenty minutes, my lord Vizier. I have the briefing documents here. They're -- well, they're organized, mostly. The page about the northern border is somewhat -- I spilled tea on it. The important numbers are still legible. Mostly."
2. "My lord Vizier, I want you to know that I am completely, absolutely, unwaveringly loyal. I was loyal to the last Vizier and I am loyal to you and I will be loyal to the next -- no, that sounded wrong. There won't be a next. I mean, not because you'll -- I mean you'll serve for many years. I should stop talking."
3. "Actually, my lord Vizier, regulation 112 of the Trade Code, ratified under Emperor Darius II in the Year of the Copper Moon, specifically addresses this exact situation. If we invoke it, the Guild has no legal standing for their position. I can draft the order within the hour. The filing system is one thing I am very, very good at."

### Leila (27, court poet, romance option)

**Voice rules:**
- Poetic but never pretentious -- her verse is elaborate, her speech is direct
- Sharp wit, quick to joke
- Vivid imagery in casual conversation
- Flirtatious through wordplay, not directness
- When serious, her playfulness drops and her voice becomes quiet and intense
- Quotes poetry (her own and others') naturally

**Examples:**
1. "You want me to write a poem for the Emperor's birthday? I can write a poem about anything. The question is whether you want a good poem or a flattering poem. They are almost never the same thing."
2. "My father used to say that a poem should fit in the palm of your hand and weigh as much as the world. I think about that when I write. Mostly I think about it when I'm stuck on a second line."
3. "You're looking at me like I'm a puzzle. That's fine. Most people look at me like I'm a decoration. A puzzle is an improvement."

### Navid (30, military captain, romance option)

**Voice rules:**
- Formal, military register -- short declarative sentences
- Gradually warms (sentences get longer, more personal over time)
- Uses proper address until the relationship deepens, then switches to the player's name
- Describes things in concrete, physical terms
- Subtle humor, easy to miss
- Deflects emotion into practicality

**Examples:**
1. "The perimeter is secure. Two guards on each entrance, one on the roof. Nobody enters without authorization. That includes you, Vizier, until I've confirmed the area is safe. Apologies."
2. "I don't talk much. People mistake that for not thinking. I think constantly. I just don't see the point of saying most of it out loud. Present company... is beginning to change my mind about that."
3. "This scar? Khanate raider. Two years ago, at the Darband pass. He was fast. I was faster. But not fast enough to avoid his blade entirely. I keep the scar to remind myself that 'faster' is a relative term."

### Asha (26, temple scholar, romance option)

**Voice rules:**
- Questions -- she asks more than she states
- Teasing, playful, sometimes provocative
- Sudden shifts from playfulness to deep seriousness
- Socratic method (leads people to conclusions through questions)
- Laughs easily and often
- Becomes still and quiet when moved (striking contrast to her usual animation)

**Examples:**
1. "If the Third Wisdom says courage is acting despite fear, does that mean a person who fears nothing cannot be courageous? Is fear a prerequisite for bravery? I have been arguing this with the Masters for three years and none of them will give me a straight answer."
2. "You came to the temple. Interesting. Most Viziers treat the temple as a formality -- bow to the Flame, nod at the High Priest, leave as quickly as possible. You stayed. Why? Don't answer with what you think I want to hear. I hate that."
3. "I laugh a lot. People think I'm not serious. But laughter and seriousness are not opposites, Vizier. They're complements. The person who cannot laugh at the absurdity of existence has no business trying to understand it."

## CHOICE DESIGN

### The 70/30 Rule

- **70% fake choices** -- shape personality, build relationships, set flavor text. Do not branch the plot.
- **30% real choices** -- change the story direction using `*goto`, `*goto_scene`, or `*finish`.

This ratio is essential for managing content scope. In a 300,000-word game, writing every branch of every real choice already produces enormous content. Fake choices keep the reader engaged without exponential content growth.

### Making Fake Choices Feel Real

1. **Immediate variation.** The paragraph following a fake choice should vary based on the selection (use `*temp` to track within the scene).
2. **NPC reactions.** NPCs should reference the player's personality in later scenes (use personality axis checks).
3. **Gated options.** Later choices should be gated behind personality thresholds (using `*selectable_if`).
4. **Stat visibility.** Players can see their personality changing in the stat screen.

```
*fake_choice
  #"The border must be defended at any cost."
    *set boldness %+ 10
    *set ruthless %+ 5
    *temp response "aggressive"
  #"We should negotiate before we fight."
    *set idealist %+ 5
    *set honest %+ 5
    *temp response "diplomatic"
  #"We need more information before we act."
    *set boldness %- 5
    *temp response "cautious"

*if (response = "aggressive")
  General Kadir nods, a rare gesture of approval. "Finally. Someone who understands."
*elseif (response = "diplomatic")
  High Priest Ashoka inclines his head. "Wisdom speaks softly, but it speaks."
*else
  Spymaster Zara's expression does not change, but you could swear you see the ghost of a smile.
```

### Choice Option Design

- **Always 3-4 options.** Never 2 (feels binary). Rarely 5+ (overwhelms the reader).
- **Each option should be distinct in approach**, not just wording. Different options should reflect different personality axes, skills, or strategies.
- **Label options clearly.** If an option requires a skill or personality threshold, show it in brackets.
- **The "safe" option should always be available.** Never lock the player out of all good choices.

**Option types:**

| Type | Example | What it tests |
|---|---|---|
| Bold/direct | "I'll handle this myself." | Boldness, confidence |
| Diplomatic/careful | "Let's discuss this calmly." | Diplomacy, caution |
| Clever/indirect | "I know something they don't." | Cunning, deception |
| Scholarly/analytical | "I've read about this situation." | Scholarship, knowledge |
| Emotional/personal | "This isn't about politics. It's about people." | Compassion, idealism |

### Skill-Gated Options

Use `*selectable_if` to show options the player cannot currently select. This encourages replay and rewards investment.

```
*choice
  *selectable_if (strength > 55) #Break down the door. [Requires Strength 55+]
    You put your shoulder to the heavy oak and push.
    *goto force_entry
  *selectable_if (cunning > 40) #Pick the lock. [Requires Cunning 40+]
    You pull a thin piece of wire from your sleeve.
    *goto pick_lock
  #Look for another way in.
    You step back and examine the wall.
    *goto search_wall
```

## CHOICESCRIPT TECHNICAL RULES

### Indentation

- **2-space indentation.** Consistent throughout all files.
- Indent code under `*choice` options, `*if` blocks, and `*label` subroutines.
- Never use tabs.

```
*choice
  #First option.
    This is text under the first option.
    *set variable + 1
    *goto label_name
  #Second option.
    *if (condition)
      Conditional text.
    *else
      Other text.
    *goto other_label
```

### Variable Declarations

- `*create` only in startup.txt, before any narrative text.
- `*temp` for scene-local variables in any file.
- Variable names: `snake_case`, lowercase, descriptive.
- Label names: `snake_case`, lowercase, descriptive.

### Flow Control Rules

- Every `*choice` option MUST end with `*goto`, `*goto_scene`, `*finish`, or `*ending`.
- Every `*fake_choice` option does NOT need an exit command. Text continues after the block.
- Every code path must terminate. A scene without `*choice`, `*finish`, `*goto_scene`, or `*ending` at the bottom will crash.
- Use `*bug "message"` for code paths that should be unreachable. This catches logic errors during testing.

### Page Breaks

Use `*page_break` every 200-400 words. Rules:

- **Before every `*choice` and `*fake_choice`.** Give the reader a moment before deciding.
- **After major events.** Let the reader breathe after action scenes or revelations.
- **Between scene segments.** Natural pause points in the narrative.
- **Never more than 500 words without a break.** Anything longer feels like a wall of text on a phone screen.
- **After a choice, keep the next page SHORT (100-200 words)** to show consequences quickly.

### Fairmath vs. Arithmetic

| Use Fairmath (`%+` / `%-`) | Use Arithmetic (`+` / `-`) |
|---|---|
| Personality axes | Skills |
| Relationships | Gold |
| Faction reputation | Counters (conversations, evidence) |
| | Route/ending (direct `*set`) |

### Pronoun Usage

All narrative text must use pronoun variables:

```
${they} entered the room and removed ${their} cloak.
"Is that ${theirs}?" the guard asked, pointing at the sword.
${theyre} not sure what to make of this situation.
The young ${person} stood ${their} ground.
```

Never hardcode gendered pronouns for the player character. Always use `${variable}`.

### Variable Interpolation

Use `${variable}` for inline display:

```
"Welcome, ${player_name}," the Emperor says.
Your strength is ${strength}.
```

Use `@{}` for multireplace:

```
@{gender He|She|They} nodded.
@{route The loyalist path stretches before you.|Power is within your grasp.|The people cry out for change.}
```

### Testing Compatibility

Include the randomtest bypass for `*input_text`:

```
*if (choice_randomtest)
  *set player_name "TestPlayer"
*else
  What is your name?
  *input_text player_name
```

### Comment Conventions

Use `*comment` liberally for:
- Section headers within long files
- Explaining complex conditional logic
- Marking TODO items
- Noting which variables a section modifies

```
*comment === SCENE 3: THE COUNCIL MEETING ===
*comment Variables modified: met_general, met_guild, scene3_crisis_response

*comment TODO: Add more flavor text for high-scholarship players
```

## NAMING CONVENTIONS

### Character Names

All character names are drawn from Persian, Arabic, Sanskrit, and Central Asian naming traditions to reflect the Khazaran cultural blend. Names should be:
- Pronounceable for English-speaking readers
- Distinct from each other (no two names starting with the same letter among major characters)
- Evocative of character personality

## CONTENT GUIDELINES

### Romance Scenes

- Romance content should be emotionally rich but not sexually explicit. The target rating is PG-13 equivalent.
- Physical intimacy is described through suggestion and metaphor, not graphic detail.
- Each romance option should feel genuinely distinct -- not the same romance with a different name.
- Romance should complement the political plot, not replace it. The best romantic moments arise from the shared danger and political tension.

### Violence

- Violence is described with consequence and weight, not glorified.
- Battle scenes emphasize the chaos, fear, and cost of combat.
- Death should feel significant. Named characters who die should be mourned.
- The player can choose violent approaches but should face moral consequences.

### Political Content

- The game explores political systems and power structures. It does not advocate for a specific political philosophy.
- Each route (Loyalist, Kingmaker, Revolutionary) is presented as having genuine merits and genuine costs.
- No route is "the right answer." The game respects the player's choice.

### Cultural Sensitivity

- The Khazaran Empire is inspired by historical Mughal, Ottoman, and Silk Road cultures but is not a direct representation of any real culture.
- Avoid stereotypes. The empire should feel like a real, complex civilization with internal diversity.
- Religious elements (the Eternal Flame faith) are fictional and should not directly parallel any real religion.
- Slavery exists in the setting and can be addressed in the Revolutionary route. Treat the subject with appropriate gravity.
